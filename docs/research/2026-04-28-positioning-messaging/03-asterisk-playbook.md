# Positioning Playbook: Asterisk Magazine

**Date:** 2026-04-28
**Analyst:** Research agent 03/10, positioning/messaging swarm
**Why this matters:** Cycle 2 visual-design research flagged Asterisk as the closest brand analog Argumend has — same audience (rationalists, EA-curious, intellectually-serious generalists), same source-citing prose ethos, a print-magazine feel that survives digital. Argumend has the *aesthetic* gestures right; what's missing is Asterisk's *positioning machine*.

## 1. Who Asterisk is

Asterisk is a quarterly magazine of long-form essays about science, AI, economics, global health, and the methodological soft underbelly of social science. Founded by **Clara Collier** (Editor in Chief), announced on the EA Forum on **November 21, 2022** with the inaugural issue arriving the same week ([EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/YdHMxWKBaa79JcsSW/announcing-the-first-issue-of-asterisk)). Collier had been editing in and around the rationalist/EA scene as `claracollieredits` on Substack; the magazine grew out of her sense that "a lot of the blogs I was reading...were much more transparent about how they were constructing stories on quite complex topics. And this felt more interesting and vital for me, but also missing a lot of the rigor that I found in more traditional publications" ([Complex Systems podcast](https://www.complexsystemspodcast.com/episodes/building-institutions-that-bend-towards-truth-with-clara-collier-of-asterisk-magazine/)).

Masthead: Collier; **Angela Chen** (Managing Editor, ex-*MIT Tech Review* and *Wired*); print/identity by **MGMT Design** (Sarah Gephart); web by Daniel G. Siegel ([asteriskmag.com/about](https://asteriskmag.com/about)). Publishing entity is **Obelus, Inc.**, a 501(c)(3); originally fiscally sponsored by the Centre for Effective Altruism / Effective Ventures Foundation USA ([Introducing Asterisk](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/Mts84Mv5cFHRYBBA8/introducing-asterisk)).

**Funding.** Headline grant: **$937,231 from Open Philanthropy (now Coefficient Giving)** to Effective Ventures Foundation USA, earmarked for Asterisk ([Open Phil grant](https://www.openphilanthropy.org/grants/effective-ventures-foundation-usa-asterisk-magazine/)). A follow-on 2023 grant exists ([2023 grant](https://www.openphilanthropy.org/grants/effective-ventures-foundation-usa-asterisk-2023/)). Subscriptions and individual donors backfill. The about page disavows editorial influence — "full editorial independence" sits next to the Coefficient Giving credit ([asteriskmag.com/about](https://asteriskmag.com/about)).

**Circulation.** Print run undisclosed. The Substack mirror has **6,000+ subscribers** ([Substack](https://asteriskmag.substack.com/)). A separate AI vertical at `asteriskmag.ai` runs an Asterisk AI Blogging Fellowship ([Manifund](https://manifund.org/projects/asterisk-ai-blogging-fellowship)). Andrew Marantz's *New Yorker* AI safety profile name-dropped Asterisk as "a handsomely designed print magazine." Issues sell on `store.asteriskmag.com`.

For Argumend's purposes: **a single founder, ~$1M philanthropic runway, a five-person editorial loop, 14 issues in ~3.5 years.** Reproducible. Not a media giant — a peer-scale operation that out-positioned its peer set.

## 2. Asterisk's category claim

The sharpest move Asterisk made was refusing to be a Substack.

It *publishes* on Substack, but it is structurally and rhetorically a **magazine**. Four numbered, themed issues per year. A printer deadline. 120 fixed pages. A masthead with Editor in Chief and Managing Editor. Cover art. An "Issues" back-catalog ([asteriskmag.com/issues](https://asteriskmag.com/issues)). `store.asteriskmag.com` for back issues. A *shorter-form* blog called **Dagger** explicitly subordinated to the magazine — "Dagger is Asterisk's new home for timelier takes, short essays, and the occasional provocation between quarterly issues."

The category claim does several things at once:

- **Conscripts respect upfront.** A magazine is a thing people commission, copy-edit, and print. A Substack is, by default, a guy. Asterisk borrows magazines' priors (deliberate, edited, considered) without earning them per-piece.
- **Manufactures release events.** A "new issue" is a PR moment. Each issue gets its own EA Forum announcement, X launch tweet, and ACX shout-out. Scott Alexander's [Issue 2 of Asterisk](https://www.astralcodexten.com/p/issue-two-of-asterisk) is a free issue-launch ad to ~150K ACX readers. Substacks don't get launch events; magazines do.
- **Enables themed batching.** Issue 2 was Food. Issue 3 was AI. Issue 6 was California. Issue 14 is Risk. The theme is the editorial gravity well — it commissions writers, recruits illustrators, and gives readers a reason to read pieces they'd skip individually. A blog publishing "the life of white oak railroad ties" is eccentric. A *Risk* issue with that piece is *interesting*.
- **Justifies design budget.** Magazines have illustrators. Substacks have stock images. Asterisk's MGMT Design partnership won an AIGA 360 award ([MGMT Design / Asterisk](https://mgmtdesign.com/work/asterisk-magazine)). Inside the magazine frame, it reads as *correct*.

The peer set Asterisk implicitly competes with — Slow Boring, Marginal Revolution, ACX, Works in Progress, Persuasion — are almost all Substacks. By stepping sideways into "magazine," Asterisk differentiated without having to beat them at volume.

## 3. The 1-liner / tagline evolution

Asterisk has cycled through several taglines. Tracking them is useful because each reveals a sharper positioning instinct.

- **Launch (Nov 2022, EA Forum):** "a new quarterly journal of clear writing and clear thinking about things that matter (and, occasionally, things we just think are interesting)" ([EA Forum announcement](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/YdHMxWKBaa79JcsSW/announcing-the-first-issue-of-asterisk)).
- **Original About-page line (2022):** "for Bayes' theorem, acknowledging our uncertainty, weird hypotheticals, and well constructed sentences" ([Introducing Asterisk](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/Mts84Mv5cFHRYBBA8/introducing-asterisk)).
- **Current top-of-page identity (2026):** "The little magazine of the San Francisco Bay Area — which is to say, we're the little magazine of the world, a few years in advance" ([asteriskmag.com/about](https://asteriskmag.com/about)).
- **Current Substack tagline:** "Clear writing and clear thinking about the worlds shaping the Bay Area. New pieces on Wednesdays" ([Substack](https://asteriskmag.substack.com/)).

Decompose the strongest of these — the current Bay Area line:

- **Who:** "The little magazine" — an aesthetic admission of scale that flatters readers ("you're the kind of person who reads little magazines"). Borrows from the *n+1*, *Granta*, *The Baffler* lineage.
- **How:** "of the San Francisco Bay Area" — specific, geographic, defensible. Not "of the rationalist community," not "of effective altruism." The Bay Area is a synecdoche for the actual subject matter (AI, biotech, forecasting, progress studies) without naming any of it.
- **Why-it-matters:** "the little magazine of the world, a few years in advance" — claims temporal vanguard status without saying "futurist." Implies the Bay Area is an early-warning system for the rest of the world. This is a *soft* version of "we are the EA magazine" that EA-skeptics can read past.

The launch line "clear writing and clear thinking about things that matter" is also strong — it does the work of a tagline by stating the *editorial promise* (clarity) and the *subject filter* (mattering) in fourteen words. Compare to weaker hypotheticals Asterisk could have written and didn't:

- *"A magazine for the effective altruism community"* — would have ghettoized them.
- *"Long-form journalism on existential risk"* — would have collapsed them into LessWrong.
- *"Smart writing for smart people"* — would have read as smug Substack-ese.
- *"The official magazine of rationalism"* — would have lost the EA-skeptical reader on contact.

The pattern: **Asterisk consistently picks taglines that describe the editorial process (clear thinking, deeply researched, obsessively curious) rather than the audience (EAs, rationalists).** The audience self-identifies through the process description. This is the move Argumend's current homepage hero does *not* yet make.

## 4. Visual signature

Cycle 2's visual-design research file 06 flagged Asterisk's signature as the template to copy. The mechanic:

- **Hand-drawn asterisk per issue.** Per MGMT: "Each issue features a unique hand-drawn asterisk. These symbols serve as end marks throughout the magazine, paired with the publication's bold sans serif logotype" ([MGMT Design](https://mgmtdesign.com/work/asterisk-magazine)). One symbol is brand mark, cover art, *and* in-line glyph. New illustration per issue keeps the brand alive without a redesign — consistency is "an asterisk on the cover," variety is "*which* asterisk."
- **Typography.** Bold sans-serif logotype, serif body. Print commits harder to book typography; web is restrained sans + serif body. Notably *not* the SaaS Inter+Inter+Inter default.
- **Cover illustrations per issue.** Stated influence chain: "'70s science fiction, '80s nuclear disarmament publications, William Hogarth illustrations, and 18th century book design." Three of four reference points are pre-1990 — deliberately not contemporary, which is why Asterisk doesn't look like every tech-adjacent publication launched in the last five years.
- **Color.** Website uses a rotating palette tied to the current issue. Digital echo of the print convention.
- **The respectability play.** Asterisk's signature is *boring* in tech-aesthetic terms. Not animated. No scroll-jack. No 3D hero, particle system, "scroll to reveal." It's — deliberately — the digital reproduction of a thing you could pick up at McNally Jackson. **That's why it lands.** It signals: not trying to win you over with motion design; trying to win you over with sentences. The audience self-selects on that signal.

For Argumend: the existing stoic/parchment aesthetic is correct. Missing piece is the *rotating motif* — one illustrated mark that changes per topic-batch (or "issue") while staying structurally identical. Cycle 2's natural-philosophy-notebook proposal lines up cleanly.

## 5. Editorial voice

A typical Asterisk piece is **~4,000 words**, footnoted, evidence-rich, anti-clickbait. Each issue clusters around a theme. Themes to date ([asteriskmag.com/issues](https://asteriskmag.com/issues)):

| # | Theme | Date |
|---|---|---|
| 14 | Risk | May 2026 |
| 13 | Science | January 2026 |
| 12 | Books | October 2025 |
| 11 | Church & State | July 2025 |
| 10 | Origins | May 2025 |
| 9 | Weird | January 2025 |
| 8 | Communities | November 2024 |
| 7 | Development | July 2024 |
| 6 | California | May 2024 |
| 5 | Mistakes | February 2024 |
| 4 | Measurement | October 2023 |
| 3 | AI | July 2023 |
| 2 | Food | March 2023 |
| 1 | Inaugural | November 2022 |

The voice slot is occupied: serious without being academic, curious without being credulous, willing to say "we don't know" out loud. The about page principles include "Probabilistic" ("acknowledging that we can't be absolutely sure of anything") and "Obsessively curious" ("asking 'wait — but why?'") ([asteriskmag.com/about](https://asteriskmag.com/about)). The magazine reliably publishes pieces with named expert disagreement — Issue 6's "The EA-Progress Studies War is Here, and It's a Constructive Dialogue!" is the canonical example ([Issue 6](https://asteriskmag.com/issues/06/the-ea-progress-studies-war-is-here-and-its-a-constructive-dialogue)).

The peer publications occupying adjacent slots are *The New Atlantis* (more conservative, more bioethics), *City Journal* (urban policy, right-of-center), *Persuasion* (Yascha Mounk's centrist defense-of-liberalism Substack), and *Works in Progress* (the closest analog — Stripe Press / progress-studies adjacent, also free print, also themed issues). Asterisk threads the needle by being EA-adjacent without being a EA-recruiting venue. As one Hacker News commenter put it bluntly: "Asterisk magazine has much more vivid, down-to-earth writing than 'bay area rationalist' blog posts" ([HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=38804817)).

The unwritten internal description, evident from reading any three issues back to back: **Asterisk is an Effective Altruism magazine that doesn't say "EA" on the cover.** That is the move. It lets the magazine recruit non-EA writers (the masthead and contributors page name many), capture non-EA readers, and avoid getting trapped inside the FTX-aftermath EA reputational tax — while still anchoring funding and audience in the community that started it.

## 6. Audience-acquisition mechanics

Asterisk's growth path is short and reproducible:

1. **EA Forum announcement** at launch and per-issue ([Introducing Asterisk](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/Mts84Mv5cFHRYBBA8/introducing-asterisk), [Issue 1 announcement](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/YdHMxWKBaa79JcsSW/announcing-the-first-issue-of-asterisk)). Zero cost.
2. **ACX shout-out** ([Issue Two of Asterisk](https://www.astralcodexten.com/p/issue-two-of-asterisk), 65 likes, 54 shares). Scott Alexander treats Asterisk as an extension of his recommendation circle.
3. **X** (`@asteriskmgzn`, joined Nov 2022) for issue launches; pieces that travel get reposted by Bay Area / SF rationalist orbit writers.
4. **Substack mirror** — 6,000+ subs, free, email-capture funnel that doesn't require print commitment on first contact.
5. **Print subscription as upgrade tier** at `store.asteriskmag.com`.
6. **Podcast appearances** — Patrick McKenzie's *Complex Systems* hosted Collier; the interview itself is a positioning artifact, re-circulating as quotes.
7. **Press cameos** — Andrew Marantz dropped Asterisk into the *New Yorker* "Among the Doomsayers" AI safety profile.
8. **Specific viral pieces** — Issue 8's "The Making of Community Notes" and "The Depths of Wikipedians" broke containment beyond the EA orbit. Pattern: when a piece travels, it's because it's *the* definitive long-form piece on a topic Twitter is currently arguing about.
9. **Spinout properties** — `asteriskmag.ai` AI vertical and an AI Blogging Fellowship via [Manifund](https://manifund.org/projects/asterisk-ai-blogging-fellowship).

The chain is **Asterisk → ACX → Twitter → website** — four hops, each with conversion. Argumend has none of those hops wired.

## 7. What Argumend can copy

Concretely, in priority order:

**The "issues" rhythm.** Argumend already has 109 pre-analyzed topics. Group them into themed batches and call those batches **volumes** or **issues**. "Volume 1: AI Safety," "Volume 2: Drug Policy," "Volume 3: Energy." Each volume gets a release event (X thread, blog post, email blast), a landing page, and a cover illustration. A topic landing inside `/volumes/02-drug-policy/topics/decriminalization-vs-legalization` reads as *editorially curated*, not algorithmically generated. This is free positioning lift.

**The print-magazine respectability vocabulary.** Argumend doesn't need print. It needs the format vocabulary: Editor's Note per volume, Contributors page (AI agents + human reviewers + cited experts), Letters (reader pushback), Errata (high-credibility move for an LLM-driven tool). Signals editorial seriousness regardless of substrate.

**The hand-drawn rotating motif.** Cycle 2 already recommended a natural-philosophy-notebook signature. Asterisk's playbook validates this: one mark, redrawn per volume, used as both header and end-of-section glyph. For Argumend, the natural mark is the **node-with-edges** or the **branching argument graph** — abstracted to one or two strokes, redrawn for each topic batch. Same skeleton, different illustration. Boring and consistent at the system level, fresh at the issue level.

**The serious-not-cute editorial voice.** Argumend's homepage prose, blog posts, and topic descriptions should aspire to Asterisk-tier seriousness. Read the first paragraph of any Issue 14 piece, then read Argumend's homepage hero. Asterisk's first paragraph respects the reader; the typical SaaS hero performs at the reader. This is a copy edit, not a redesign.

**The Open Phil + ACX adjacency.** Asterisk's $937K Open Philanthropy grant is a hard-to-replicate moat — but the *type* of relationship is replicable. Argumend's funding story should be coherent enough that Coefficient Giving / SFF / the LTFF / Manifund could plausibly underwrite topic-batch commissioning the way they underwrote Asterisk's editorial commissioning. ACX adjacency requires (a) a piece worth Scott Alexander linking to and (b) being legibly part of the rationalist intellectual project. Argumend's "AI safety arguments" volume is the most direct path to both.

**The themed batches.** Asterisk's quarterly cadence is a forcing function. A "volume per quarter" cadence for Argumend would force editorial discipline (you must finish 10 topics by date X), produce shippable PR moments (volume launches), and create a reason for past readers to come back (next volume drops Tuesday).

## 8. What Argumend should NOT copy

Asterisk is a publication. Argumend is a tool. Tools live or die on retention loops, not on essay quality. A few lines should not be crossed:

- **Don't go print.** Print fits Asterisk because the artifact *is* the product. The Argumend artifact is the interactive graph; rendering it as ink is a downgrade. Adopt the vocabulary, not the substrate.
- **Don't replace interactivity with prose.** The temptation, after reading 14 issues of Asterisk, is to make Argumend topic pages read like Asterisk articles. Resist. The graph is the value. Prose framing is wrapper.
- **Don't go quarterly only.** Asterisk's slowness is a feature given its category. Argumend can ship topics weekly inside a volume frame; volumes can release quarterly while individual topics drop continuously.
- **Don't disclaim funding the way Asterisk does.** Asterisk's funding paragraph is structured around editorial independence from a $1M grant. Argumend's funding story is a solo founder. Don't overstate.
- **Don't out-EA the audience.** Asterisk has been careful to keep "EA" off the cover. Argumend should keep "AI-powered argument mapping" off the cover too — at least off the *primary* hero. Lead with the controversies, not the technology.

## 9. Concrete adoption plan — 5 moves in next 90 days

**Move 1 — Restructure 109 topics into 6-10 volumes.** Group existing topics by theme (AI Risk, Drug Policy, Climate, Education, Health, Geopolitics, Tech Ethics, Economics). Build a `/volumes` index page that mirrors `asteriskmag.com/issues`. Add volume metadata to each topic. ~1 week of structural work, mostly in `data/topics/` and a new route.

**Move 2 — Commission a per-volume mark.** One illustrator, one brief: a node-and-edge graph stylized as natural-philosophy-notebook ink, one variant per volume. Eight illustrations. This is the Asterisk move applied. Use the mark as cover, header, and end-of-section glyph.

**Move 3 — Rewrite the homepage hero in Asterisk voice.** Strip the SaaS verbs. Replace with editorial-promise + subject-filter pattern. Working draft: "A graph atlas of clear thinking on hard topics." (or similar — the structure is editorial-promise, subject-filter, no audience-naming, no technology-naming.) A/B against the current copy.

**Move 4 — Launch a Volume 1 release event.** Pick the strongest existing batch (likely AI Safety). Write an Editor's Note. Write a 1500-word essay that synthesizes the volume's takeaways. Tweet thread it. Post it on EA Forum and LessWrong. Email to anyone in Argumend's existing list. Ask three rationalist-adjacent newsletter writers (ACX recommendations list is a starting point) to link it. The cost is one weekend of writing.

**Move 5 — Stand up Dagger.** Asterisk's Dagger blog is the short-form between-issues channel. Argumend already has a `/blog`. Rename and reframe it explicitly as the "between volumes" channel — short pieces, weekly cadence, timelier takes. Tie its visual identity to the rotating volume mark. The blog already exists; this is a positioning relabel, not a build.
