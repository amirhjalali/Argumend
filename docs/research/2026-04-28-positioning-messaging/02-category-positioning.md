# Category Positioning: Is "Argument-Mapping Platform" the Right Label?

**Date:** 2026-04-28
**Analyst:** Research agent 02/10, positioning/messaging swarm
**Question:** What category does Argumend claim — and is the current claim a positioning trap?

## 1. Why category matters

Category isn't decoration on a positioning statement. It is the *frame* a buyer uses to decide what your product is, what it should cost, what features it should have, and who it competes with. Pick the wrong category and a brilliant product gets compared to the wrong reference set, priced against the wrong anchor, and rejected for failing tests it was never built to pass.

April Dunford makes this point repeatedly across *Obviously Awesome* and her follow-up writing: "Your market category is essential because customers have an implicit set of expectations around price, features, and service level and will generally choose the 'best' solution in that category. Market category is another way of saying, 'What is the context I can wrap around my product that makes this value obvious to these people?'" ([April Dunford — Understanding the Job of a Market Category](https://aprildunford.substack.com/p/understanding-the-job-of-a-market), [aprildunford.com — Quickstart Guide](https://www.aprildunford.com/post/a-quickstart-guide-to-positioning)). She gives three viable styles: head-to-head leadership, dominate-a-subsegment, and category creation — and warns that the third is the riskiest. In her PANBlast interview she notes that **roughly 90% of B2B tech companies that went public in the last five years were positioned in existing categories, not new ones** — so for most products, "trying to create a category is a death sentence because it is an expensive, ego-driven trap that burns cash and confuses buyers" ([PANBlast — It's a Crapshoot](https://www.panblastpr.com/resources/category-creation-positioning-april-dunford/)).

Christopher Lochhead's *Play Bigger* school argues the opposite extreme: the legendary outcomes — Salesforce, Uber, Amazon, IKEA — come from inventing the category, not winning a slot inside one. "You can't build a legendary company without building a legendary category" ([summary](https://www.categorydesignadvisors.com/play-bigger/), [Marketing Journal](https://www.marketingjournal.org/play-bigger-an-interview-with-christopher-lochhead/)). The Magic Triangle — product, company, and category designed simultaneously — is the discipline they sell.

Eddie Yoon's HBR pieces ("Why It Pays to Be a Category Creator," 2013; "Make Your Best Customers Even Better," 2014) reconcile the two by anchoring category creation in *superconsumer* demand: superconsumers — ~10% of customers — drive 30–70% of sales, and the only category creation that pays off is one those superconsumers were already trying to invent for themselves ([HBR 2013](https://hbr.org/2013/03/why-it-pays-to-be-a-category-creator), [HBR 2014](https://hbr.org/2014/03/make-your-best-customers-even-better)).

The synthesis for Argumend, pre-traction, no proven superconsumer cohort: **default to fitting an existing category the wedge audience already understands. Only attempt creation if no existing category fits and a superconsumer base is visible.** Anything else is the Fluflommer trap — spend years teaching the market what your made-up word means before selling a single thing ([Apricot Studio](https://www.apricot-studio.com/blog/category-vs-positioning-when-you-should-create-a-new-lane)).

## 2. Argumend's possible categories

Nine candidates, scored quickly:

**A. Argument-mapping platform (current).** Niche academic-coded. Competitors: Kialo, Argdown, Rationale, Arguman, Cmap. Buyer: philosophy/composition/critical-thinking instructors. Search volume for "argument map" is too low to register on Google Trends, which returns zero for under-threshold terms ([Trends FAQ](https://support.google.com/trends/answer/4365533?hl=en)). TAM small, growth flat, competitor density high relative to demand. Strength: maker-true. Weakness: dry, smells of homework.

**B. Debate visualizer.** Adjacent: NYT/Guardian interactive debates, ProCon.org, Intelligence Squared. Not really a category — these are *editorial features* inside publications. TAM as a SaaS line item: zero. Trap: sounds like a Tableau plugin.

**C. Epistemic infrastructure.** Rationalist-coded. Aspirational. Not a buyer-facing category — no procurement officer issues an RFP for "epistemic infrastructure." Useful as tribe-signaling inside ACX/LW (where "AI for epistemics" is now a hackathon topic — [LessWrong](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/Gi8NP9CMwJMMSCWvc/ai-for-epistemics-hackathon)) but illegible outside. Useful in DMs, useless in headlines.

**D. Civic tech / democratic discourse tool.** Pol.is adjacent. Grant-friendly (Knight, Omidyar, Open Philanthropy), occasionally government-friendly (vTaiwan, Decidim). Buyer: program officers, gov innovation labs, civic-tech nonprofits. **TAM is grants, not seats.** Pol.is is the cautionary tale: cited everywhere, deployed in Taiwan with real legislative outcomes ([MIT Tech Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2018/08/21/240284/the-simple-but-ingenious-system-taiwan-uses-to-crowdsource-its-laws/), [compdemocracy](https://compdemocracy.org/case-studies/2014-vtaiwan/)), commercially fragile (see §3).

**E. AI research / analysis tool.** Perplexity adjacent. Broad market but hard to differentiate. Competitors: Perplexity, Elicit, Consensus, Glasp, ChatGPT. TAM huge, competitor density brutal. Argumend gets lost without a sharper sub-slice.

**F. News literacy / media literacy tool.** AllSides (511K MAU), Ground News (~30K stories/day from 40K sources), NewsGuard (7,500+ rated sites, 800+ libraries) ([Albis comparison](https://www.albis.news/lens/ground-news-vs-allsides-vs-albis-comparison)). 2024 Edelman Trust Barometer put media literacy in top-5 senior-leadership skills. AllSides ran a Wefunder round with revenue accelerating into 2026 ([Wefunder](https://wefunder.com/allsides)). TAM real and growing. Buyer: consumers, schools, libraries, corporate L&D. Reframe required: from "we map arguments" to "we help you read controversies critically."

**G. Critical thinking education tool.** Kialo Edu adjacent. 18+ month sales cycle. Buyer: teachers, curriculum directors. Kialo Edu hit ~1M users by 2025 with 60+ staff and a foundation funder — that's the ceiling shape. Argumend can't out-Kialo Kialo at K-12 without LTI 1.3, Moodle/Canvas integration, BETT/ISTE booths (per cycle 1 intel). Viable as an IB TOK / AP Seminar wedge but forecloses the rationalist audience as a *primary* claim.

**H. Public reasoning / structured discourse (new).** Risky but distinctive. Some legs in rationalist-civic intersection. No buyer yet. Per Dunford, death-sentence quadrant unless a superconsumer base materializes.

**I. Decision-support tool.** Notion AI / Cursor adjacent. Broad. Competitor density maximal. Competing against general-purpose LLM chat is suicidal without a sharper hook.

## 3. The Pol.is parallel

Pol.is is the most relevant cautionary tale in the dataset because it shares Argumend's structural problem: a genuinely beloved, intellectually serious tool that the world has *celebrated* without being willing to *pay for*.

Colin Megill and team built Pol.is in response to communication breakdowns observed in Occupy Wall Street and the Arab Spring ([colinmegill.com](https://colinmegill.com/), [Wikipedia — Pol.is](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pol.is)). The tool hides divisive comments and elevates statements that earn agreement across opinion clusters — anti-engagement-bait by design. Audrey Tang adopted it for vTaiwan in 2015, where it produced binding policy on UberX, fintech sandboxes, and online alcohol sales ([MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2018/08/21/240284/the-simple-but-ingenious-system-taiwan-uses-to-crowdsource-its-laws/), [HBR podcast with Tang](https://hbr.org/podcast/2020/10/how-taiwan-is-using-technology-to-foster-democracy-with-digital-minister-audrey-tang)). Tang has called Polis "a kind of social media that instead of polarizing people to drive engagement automatically drives bridge-making narratives" ([RebootDemocracy interview](https://rebootdemocracy.ai/blog/audrey-tang-ai-democracy/), [Oxford DPIR talk](https://www.politics.ox.ac.uk/event/frontier-democracy-audrey-tang-taiwans-digital-democracy-collaborative-civic-technologies-and)).

That is *immense* validation. And yet Pol.is is structurally a research project with a tiny operating team, has never had an obvious commercial flywheel, and is now mostly sustained as the **Computational Democracy Project** — a nonprofit. OpenAI, Anthropic, and others use Pol.is for "democratic AI alignment" work ([Time — Inside OpenAI's Plan to Make AI More 'Democratic'](https://time.com/6684266/openai-democracy-artificial-intelligence/)), which is prestige but not revenue.

**The Pol.is lesson for Argumend:**
1. Civic tech is grant-funded, not seat-funded. Claiming the category means competing for Knight/Omidyar/Open Philanthropy on five-year cycles — a different go-to-market than a SaaS funnel.
2. Government adoption is a credentialing event, not a revenue event. vTaiwan made Pol.is famous; it didn't make it solvent.
3. Media coverage and academic citation aren't proxies for product traction.
4. The audience that loves tools like this overlaps heavily with the audience that refuses to pay for software.

Argumend already lives partway inside this trap (offline-by-default, no paywall, foundation-friendly aesthetics). Claiming "civic tech" deepens it. Better to treat civic-tech adoption as a downstream outcome than an upstream identity.

## 4. The Asterisk Magazine parallel

Asterisk is the cleanest case of a media-shaped tribe-product that **deliberately did not call itself a platform**. From its launch in 2022, Asterisk positioned as "a quarterly magazine of clear writing about complicated questions" — funded first by Effective Ventures, later by Open Philanthropy — with an explicit print edition and a substack-then-standalone web presence ([asteriskmag.com — About](https://asteriskmag.com/about), [EA Forum — Introducing Asterisk](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/Mts84Mv5cFHRYBBA8/introducing-asterisk)).

The category claim — *magazine* — does several things at once:
- Frees them from SaaS expectations: no MAU pressure, no churn metric, no PLG funnel.
- Gives readers a familiar mental model (subscribe, read issues, share articles).
- Lets writers like Aella, Scott Alexander, Kelsey Piper, Sarah Constantin contribute without it feeling like brand-building.
- Pre-positions for nonprofit/foundation funding, which is how it's actually paid for.
- Avoids the "rationalist tool" framing that the magazine deliberately *de-emphasizes* — RationalWiki notes that articles are "full of EA and LessWrong rationalist ideas but rarely mention those movements by name" ([RationalWiki — Asterisk](https://rationalwiki.org/wiki/Asterisk_Magazine)).

**The Argumend equivalent: position as "a publication that maps controversies" rather than "an argument-mapping platform."** Tradeoffs:
- *Pro:* frees from SaaS expectations exactly the way it freed Asterisk. The 109 pre-analyzed topics become *issues*. The graph view becomes the *visual format*. Each topic page becomes an *article*.
- *Pro:* matches the actual content shape — Argumend already publishes static analyzed topics offline by default. That's a magazine, not a tool.
- *Pro:* solves the "no users, no UGC" problem by making it a feature: "We do the mapping; you read the maps."
- *Con:* magazines are even harder to monetize than SaaS at small scale.
- *Con:* loses any future optionality on the "tool" side (debate orchestrator, judge council) — though those can re-enter as "interactive sidebars" inside articles.
- *Con:* misaligned with "platform" instincts the founder already has shipped against.

This is the most underrated option in the candidate set. It is also the most Asterisk-shaped, which matters because Argumend's wedge audience overlaps Asterisk's reader audience almost perfectly.

## 5. Category creation vs. category fit

Lochhead's *Play Bigger* makes category creation the heroic narrative. Dunford's data — 90% of B2B IPOs in five years were inside-existing-category — makes it the long-tail-loser narrative. Both are right, conditional on stage.

The decision rule for a pre-traction product with no proven superconsumer cohort: **fit, don't create.** Specifically:
- *Cost of creation:* multi-year market education, repeated definitional content, expensive sales cycles, the Fluflommer drag of explaining what the category *is* before explaining why your product *wins* it ([Apricot Studio](https://www.apricot-studio.com/blog/category-vs-positioning-when-you-should-create-a-new-lane)).
- *Cost of competition:* you have to differentiate against named alternatives, which sharpens product but cedes the framing.

Dunford's nuance, important for Argumend: she doesn't believe companies *create* categories — she believes categories *emerge* and the canny company is the one that *names* the emergence early ([April Dunford Substack](https://aprildunford.substack.com/p/understanding-the-job-of-a-market)). Salesforce didn't invent SaaS; it named it. HubSpot didn't invent inbound; it named it. The trick is to spot the emerging frame and claim it before incumbents do — which is *very* different from inventing a frame that doesn't exist.

For Argumend in 2026: nothing is emerging that obviously belongs to it. "AI for epistemics" is the closest emergence — that phrase is gaining traction inside LessWrong and the LessOnline circuit ([LessWrong — AI for AI for Epistemics](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/K7tG6Fuh6pkDGHAGx/ai-for-ai-for-epistemics)). But it has no buyer, no procurement category, no comparison set yet. Naming an emergence with no buyer is still creation.

**Recommendation: take an existing category, take it sharply, and use category-naming language only as a stretch goal three years in.**

## 6. The "for whom" anchor

Categories are read by buyers, not by makers. The vocabulary the *wedge audience* uses determines what category words land.

Per cycle 1, Argumend's reachable wedge is ACX/LW/rationalist-adjacent — Astral Codex Ten readers, LessWrong commenters, Manifold/Metaculus forecasters, EA-adjacent operators, and the Twitter/X "post-rationalist" cluster. This audience's vocabulary, observed in their own writing:
- They use **"argument map"** comfortably (cf. *Tetlock-style* prediction maps, the Rationality Power Tools posts on LessWrong, [Old LW Wiki](https://old-wiki.lesswrong.com/wiki/Rationality_power_tools)).
- They use **"epistemics"** as a term of art — "improving collective epistemics" is a frequent LessWrong post topic ([LessWrong — feature idea: epistemic status](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/Hrm59GdN2yDPWbtrd/feature-idea-epistemic-status)).
- They respond to **"crux"** (Double Crux is canonical CFAR vocabulary), to **"cruxing,"** to **"steelman,"** to **"belief graph."**
- They are *allergic* to "civic tech," "deliberation platform," "media literacy" — those phrases telegraph foundation-NGO and Asterisk-readers will close the tab.

Cross-tabbing this with the candidate categories:
- *Argument map* — neutral. Understood. Slightly dry.
- *Epistemics tool* — strongly positive in-tribe, illegible out-of-tribe.
- *Public reasoning* — neutral. Slightly aspirational.
- *Civic tech* — negative in-tribe.
- *Decision support* — neutral but generic.
- *News/media literacy* — slightly negative in-tribe (perceived as middlebrow).
- *Critical thinking education* — strongly negative in-tribe.

The wedge audience essentially constrains the choice to **argument map**, **epistemics tool**, or **public-reasoning artifact**.

## 7. Recommended category

**Primary recommendation: "an epistemics tool for public controversies"** — with "argument map" as the *visible artifact* and "epistemics" as the *category gravity*.

This composes as: Argumend is an epistemics tool. It produces argument maps. The maps are about live public controversies. The audience is people who already use the word "epistemics" without explaining it.

Why not "argument-mapping platform" alone:
- "Platform" implies UGC, network effects, multi-tenant infrastructure — none of which Argumend has or needs in v1.
- "Argument-mapping" alone smells of homework. Kialo and Rationale own the dry, academic framing; competing on those terms loses.
- The category puts Argumend next to Kialo Edu in buyers' minds, and Kialo Edu is bigger, free, and has Moodle/Canvas integrations.

Why not "civic tech":
- §3 — Pol.is parallel. Grant-funded, not seat-funded. Ten-year time horizon. Misaligned with founder's stated state ("in orbit, not climbing" — needs traction, not credentialing).

Why not "news literacy":
- Slightly off-target on wedge audience. Better as a *secondary* framing for a future Ground News-shaped product line, not the primary identity.

Why not pure category creation ("public-reasoning artifact"):
- Per §5 — death-sentence quadrant for pre-traction product.
- BUT — keep it as a **stretch frame**: use the phrase in long-form essays, podcast interviews, and Asterisk-style pieces. Let it incubate. If it catches three years in, harvest it.

Why "epistemics tool" works:
- Fits the wedge audience's vocabulary natively.
- Lets argument maps, debate orchestrator, judge council, and crux extraction all sit under one roof without seeming scattered.
- Connects to the live "AI for epistemics" conversation in the rationalist sphere, which is currently the most active category-emergence event Argumend could try to ride.
- Asterisk-adjacent in tone — serious, in-tribe, not edtech-coded.

**The full positioning sentence (per Dunford's template):** *"For [people who care about thinking clearly about contested public questions], Argumend is [an epistemics tool that maps live controversies as argument graphs] — unlike [Kialo's static pro/con trees or LLM chat's plausible mush], we [extract positions, evidence, cruxes, and fallacies with named confidence and judge-council scoring]."*

## 8. Tagline templates

Composed as: "Argumend — the [category] for [audience] who want [outcome]."

1. **"Argumend — the epistemics tool for people who want to actually understand controversies, not just have opinions about them."**
2. **"Argumend — argument maps for readers who want the cruxes, not the takes."**
3. **"Argumend — a public-reasoning instrument for people allergic to vibes-based debate."**
4. **"Argumend — the controversy explorer for ACX-shaped readers who want named evidence and judge-council scoring."**
5. **"Argumend — argument maps for a world that prefers heat to light. We're trying to fix that."**

Tagline 1 is the safest in-tribe play and the strongest recommendation. Tagline 2 is the cleanest one-liner. Tagline 5 is the most personality-forward and probably the best for X/podcast intros. Tagline 4 is most explicit about the audience but is illegible outside the tribe. Tagline 3 is the most Asterisk-shaped.

## 9. Test plan

**A/B 1 — landing-page hero.** Three variants, 14 days each (or 2,000 uniques per variant): A) current "Argument maps for live controversies"; B) "An epistemics tool for live controversies"; C) "We map controversies. Read the maps." Measure scroll depth, click-through to topic page, time on first topic page, return rate.

**Founder-pitch DM test.** Send three positioning lines to ten ACX/LW-active operators with a one-line message plus link. Use the three frames above plus a "civic tech" decoy as negative control. Track replies and qualitative reactions.

**Podcast/interview framing.** On the next podcast or substack guest spot, alternate the self-description ("I built an epistemics tool" / "I built an argument-mapping platform" / "I run a publication that maps controversies"). Track which gets follow-up questions vs. polite nods.

**Search-query test.** Buy ~$200 of Google Ads against three long-tail sets — argument-map, epistemics, news-literacy. The category with lowest CPC *and* highest downstream engagement is the one Google's market is already telling you Argumend belongs to.
