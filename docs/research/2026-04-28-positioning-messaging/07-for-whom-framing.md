# Positioning: The "For Whom" Framing

**Date:** 2026-04-28
**Analyst:** Research agent 07/10, positioning/messaging swarm
**Companion to:** Cycle 1 distribution sweep, Cycle 2 file 10 ("unwedged niche-cerebral")

## 1. The "everyone" trap

Argumend's current homepage and meta description say a version of "argument mapping for controversial topics." That phrasing has no "for whom." It is the default failure mode of cerebral indie products: the founder believes the tool is universally useful, the audience for that universality is everyone, and the operational consequence is that no specific person feels addressed when they land. **The audience for "everyone" is "no one."**

This is the most-cited principle in modern positioning literature. April Dunford frames it as the "best-fit customers" component of her five-element positioning canvas — alongside competitive alternatives, differentiated capabilities, differentiated value, and market category. Dunford insists that positioning starts by asking, "What are the characteristics of a target account that make them really, really care a lot about the value they can only get from me?" Many kinds of prospects might benefit; only some will *really* care, and identifying that subset is the whole game ([April Dunford — Positioning](https://www.aprildunford.com/post/a-quickstart-guide-to-positioning), [Lenny Rachitsky — A Quickstart Guide to Positioning](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/positioning)).

Seth Godin's version is sharper. In *This Is Marketing*, he argues that "relentless pursuit of mass will make you boring, because mass means average — it requires you to try to offend no one and satisfy everyone." His operational answer is the "smallest viable audience" — the minimum number of people you need to influence for the work to be worth it ([Seth Godin — Smallest Viable Market](https://startupnation.com/books/seth-godin-smallest-viable-market/), [seths.blog](https://seths.blog/2008/03/1000-true-fans/)). Kevin Kelly's "1,000 True Fans" is the same principle in revenue clothing — find a thousand people who will spend $100/year and you have a viable creative business ([Kevin Kelly via Seth's Blog](https://seths.blog/2025/05/1000-untrue-fans/)).

Lenny Rachitsky, summarizing Dunford for the operator audience, lists three concrete moves when a product isn't taking off: "talk to more users, change your target audience/ICP, change your positioning/messaging/pitch" ([Lenny's Newsletter — Summary of April Dunford](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/summary-april-dunford-on-product)). Argumend has done the first. It has not done the other two. Cycle 2 file 10's "unwedged niche-cerebral product is functionally invisible" is a restatement of Godin: in a market where attention is rationed by relevance, refusing to specify your audience is functionally the same as having none.

The "for whom" question is therefore not a marketing-copy detail. It's a precondition for being legible at all.

## 3. The wedge audience principle

The recurring pattern in successful niche-cerebral product launches is **one primary audience, optimized for ruthlessly, with adjacent audiences accepted as spillover**. Cycle 2 file 10 named this. The canonical examples:

- **Substack** — chose paid newsletter writers, not "creators." The first cohort was a deliberately small set of writers (Bill Bishop, Andrew Sullivan, Heather Cox Richardson) whose own audiences validated the platform. Substack didn't try to be Medium-for-everyone; it built one feature set (paid subs + email) for one job (writers monetizing) ([MarTech for Humans — How Substack Did Better Than Most Startups](https://www.martechforhumans.com/p/product-market-fit-how-substack-did)).
- **Are.na** — chose designers and visual researchers, not "knowledge workers." The platform's whole aesthetic — slow, quiet, ad-free, monochrome — telegraphs "this is for people who hate Pinterest's algorithm." The wedge worked: designers brought their audiences with them.
- **Manifold Markets** — chose forecasters who already lived on LessWrong/EA Forum, not "anyone curious about prediction markets." Their first conference, Manifest 2023, was effectively a rationalist convention with Yudkowsky, Hanson, and Hanania on the docket ([Manifold — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold_(prediction_market))).
- **Roam Research** — chose networked-thinking obsessives ("Roam cult"), not "note-takers."
- **Notion** (early) — chose startup operators with strong opinions about tools, not "teams."

In each case the founder picked an audience that (i) was small enough to address directly, (ii) had a public, vocal core that other audiences listen to, and (iii) shared vocabulary that the product could speak natively. That's the wedge. Spillover is permitted; chasing spillover before owning the wedge is the failure mode.

## 4. The recommended primary audience

**Primary: (a) rationalist-adjacent / ACX-LW readers.**

The defense:

1. **Vocabulary fit is native.** Argumend's product taxonomy — positions, evidence with confidence scores, cruxes, fallacies, multi-model judges, steelmen — is *literally lifted from this community's vocabulary*. "Crux" as a first-class concept exists in Argumend because it exists in LessWrong. No other audience will see "crux crimson" highlighting and immediately understand what it means.
2. **Addressability is best-in-class.** ACX has ~6K active survey respondents and a comment section that routinely surfaces small tools. LessWrong has ~170K registered accounts but a few hundred high-signal posters. The EA Forum and AI Alignment Forum are even smaller and more concentrated. A single well-aimed ACX open-thread comment, LessWrong frontpage post, or Asterisk Magazine feature can reach the active core in days.
3. **They reject Kialo.** This is the most under-appreciated point. The 2018 LessWrong critique of Kialo ("the tree structure makes it hard to follow," "popularity voting != truth," "no good way to express conditional support") is a direct list of features Argumend's richer node taxonomy already addresses ([LessWrong — Kialo critique](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/g3odvaj8opqCF9egv/kialo-an-online-discussion-platform-that-attempts-to-support)). Argumend isn't trying to convert this audience; it's offering them the tool they've been describing for fifteen years.
4. **Spillover dynamics are favorable.** This audience writes for Asterisk, the New Atlantis, Works in Progress, Marginal Revolution, Construction Physics, Slow Boring, and dozens of mid-sized Substacks. When they cite a tool, the next-ring audiences (j Substack writers, c journalists, d policy folks) see it. Spillover from (a) → (j) → (c) is much more natural than the reverse.
5. **WTP is irrelevant at this stage.** Argumend isn't optimizing for revenue right now; it's optimizing for legibility. A free tool that 500 ACX readers actively use is worth more than one that 500K people half-remember.

**Secondary: (j) Substack writers / commentators.**

The defense for (j) over (h) EA-adjacent:

- (h) is mostly already inside the wedge — EA-adjacent readers are largely (a) by another name, with overlapping forums and vocabulary. Owning (a) effectively owns (h).
- (j) is the natural *expansion ring* — Substack writers are the people who turn the rationalist vocabulary into mainstream-adjacent content. They're the bridge from wedge to mainstream. They also have the highest medium-term WTP, since paid Substack writers do pay for research tools.
- (j) is observable. Writers are public. Their bylines are searchable. Their inboxes accept cold pitches with sample analyzed topics.

EA-adjacent (h) and AI safety (i) are best treated as *natively included* in the (a) wedge — they read the same posts and use the same words. Argumend doesn't need separate copy for them; it just needs to not exclude them.

Top three: **#2 ("steelman before they argue")**, **#6 ("update than win")**, **#1 ("find the actual disagreement")**. All three signal the wedge audience natively — "steelman" and "update" are LessWrong-native verbs — without explicitly naming a tribe, which preserves non-alienation.

#5 and #7 score high on signal but low on non-alienation. Use them on surfaces where the wedge audience is the only audience (X bio, ACX-targeted ad copy, press kit one-liner). Don't use them on the homepage.

#3 ("allergic to bad-faith") is borderline anti-positioning and belongs in section 6.

## 6. Anti-audience signaling

Counter-positioning isn't optional. Defining who the product is *not* for is what makes the for-whom credible. The brand-strategy literature is consistent on this: anti-category positioning works by identifying "what customers dislike about existing solutions and offering an alternative approach" — Duolingo against Rosetta Stone's seriousness, Heaps Normal against non-alcoholic beer's apologetics ([Mark Ritson — Versus Positioning Strategy](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/versus-positioning-strategy-take-enemy-position-against-mark-ritson)). Seth Godin's blunter version: "Everyone is not your customer. You have to learn to tell people 'This is not for you' when it's not."

For Argumend, the anti-audiences worth explicit non-addressing are:

- **The hyper-partisan.** People who land on a topic page hoping the tool will confirm their priors and then leave a comment when it doesn't. The product should signal: this isn't a scoreboard. Confidence scores are not your team winning.
- **The bad-faith debater.** People who use argument tools to construct gotchas. Argumend's framing should make clear the tool is for *finding* disagreement, not winning it.
- **The conspiracy-fluent.** People for whom "evidence" is a synonym for "what I already believe." Argumend's evidence nodes with confidence scores are explicitly an answer to this — and should be advertised as such.
- **The "all sides are equal" centrist.** False-balance epistemics is not what Argumend does. Confidence scores diverge for a reason. The product should reject "both sides have a point" as a default conclusion.

Concrete anti-positioning copy for the homepage or about page:

- "Not a debate scoreboard. Not a both-sides aggregator. Not a comment section."
- "If you want to win an argument, this isn't the tool."
- "Confidence scores don't average to 50%. Some claims really are stronger."
- "We don't moderate. We map. If you want a moderator, use Kialo." (Optional — names a competitor but cleanly differentiates.)

Anti-positioning has a second function beyond exclusion: it tells the wedge audience "we see you" in a way that pure positive copy can't. A LessWrong reader who reads "if you want to win an argument, this isn't the tool" feels addressed in a way that "argument mapping for everyone" can't replicate.

## 7. Audience on the homepage — name it or signal it?

Two options:

**Option A: Name the audience explicitly.** Hero subhead reads something like "For ACX readers, LessWrong members, and people who think in cruxes." Pros: immediate self-recognition by the wedge; immediate disqualification of non-targets; reduces ambiguity for press and partners. Cons: alienates adjacent audiences (j Substack writers may not self-identify as ACX readers); creates a "tribe lock-in" perception that limits later expansion to (c) journalists or (d) policy; risks looking culty.

**Option B: Signal through vocabulary.** Hero subhead uses tribe-native verbs (steelman, update, crux) without naming the tribe. Pros: wedge audience self-identifies through vocabulary; adjacent audiences (j, c, d) feel included if the words are familiar enough; preserves brand expansion room. Cons: slower self-recognition; risk of sounding generic to people who *don't* know the vocabulary.

**Recommendation: Option B on the homepage, Option A on the /about and /press pages.** The homepage is the highest-traffic surface and needs to land for both the wedge *and* the spillover. Vocabulary signaling does that. The /about page is where the curious reader self-selects in; that's the right place to be explicit. The press kit and X bio should be most explicit — press needs a clean tribe label to write the lede, and X is where the wedge already lives.

## 8. Audience-by-surface differentiation

Map of surfaces and what each should signal:

| Surface | Audience explicitness | Recommended one-liner |
|---|---|---|
| **Homepage hero** | Implicit — vocabulary signals only | "Maps the cruxes, evidence, and fallacies in any argument. For people who steelman before they argue." |
| **Homepage subhead** | Implicit | "Find the actual disagreement, not the noise." |
| **/about page** | Explicit but inclusive | "Argumend was built for the rationalist-adjacent reader — ACX, LessWrong, EA Forum, and the broader penumbra of people who think in cruxes. It works for anyone who reads carefully, but those are the people we built it for first." |
| **/press kit** | Most explicit | "Argumend is an argument-mapping tool for the rationalist-adjacent reader. Its primary audience overlaps heavily with Astral Codex Ten, LessWrong, the EA Forum, and Asterisk Magazine subscribers." |
| **X bio** | Tribe shorthand | "Cruxes, fallacies, evidence — for the LW/ACX-adjacent reader. argumend.org" |
| **ACX-targeted ad copy** (if ever used) | Explicit, native | "A LessWrong-style tool for the rest of the internet's arguments." |
| **Edu/teacher-targeted page** (if/when shipped) | Different audience entirely — separate page | "For IB TOK and AP Seminar teachers who want AI-graded argument maps." |
| **Substack/writer outreach DMs** | Tool-utility framing | "I built this for writers who need to map the cruxes of an issue before publishing. Want a free analyzed map of [topic]?" |

The differentiation principle: **the homepage is the lowest-commitment surface and should be the most inclusive in tone; deeper surfaces (about, press, DMs) earn the right to be more specific because the visitor has already self-selected.**

This is also how Substack handles it. The Substack homepage doesn't say "for paid newsletter writers"; it says "the home for great writing." But every press piece, every founder talk, every onboarding flow makes clear the platform is built around the writer-monetization wedge. The homepage is the welcome mat; the deeper surfaces declare the tribe.

## 9. Long-term audience expansion path

Once the wedge is owned — defined as "Argumend is the obvious tool to share when an argument-mapping topic comes up on ACX/LW/EA Forum" — expansion follows a predictable ring structure:

**Ring 1 (months 0-6): Wedge.** (a) rationalist-adjacent. KPI: 100+ unprompted shares per quarter on ACX/LW/EA Forum/X-rationalist. **Stage gate to Ring 2:** at least one ACX open-thread comment that calls Argumend "useful" without the founder posting it.

**Ring 2 (months 6-12): Adjacent overlaps.** (h) EA, (i) AI safety, (j) Substack writers. These share infrastructure with Ring 1 and require minimal repositioning — mostly outreach to specific writers with sample analyzed topics. KPI: 5+ Substack pieces that cite an Argumend topic page. **Stage gate to Ring 3:** Asterisk Magazine or a comparable mid-tier magazine writes about the tool unprompted.

**Ring 3 (year 1-2): Cerebral mainstream.** (c) explainer-beat journalists, (d) think-tank researchers, (b) academic argumentation-research community. This requires real outreach — press kits, conference attendance, possibly an academic paper or two. KPI: cited in a piece in The Atlantic, Vox, or an equivalent. **Stage gate to Ring 4:** institutional inquiry from a think tank or newsroom.

**Ring 4 (year 2-3): Education vertical.** (e) educators, (g) debate students. Requires a real product investment — LTI 1.3, Canvas/Moodle integrations, classroom features, FERPA/COPPA compliance. This is where Argumend either stays a tool for ideas-people or pivots into edtech. **Stage gate to Ring 5:** decision point — does Argumend want to be Kialo Edu's competitor, or stay in the cerebral lane?

**Ring 5 (year 3+): Mainstream curious citizen.** (f). Only reachable after Rings 1-3 have established credibility and the brand has earned enough trust signals to land with a non-self-selected audience. The Wikipedia-of-arguments vision lives here. Most products that swing for Ring 5 first die before reaching Ring 1.

The rule: **don't skip rings.** Every product that tried to launch at Ring 5 ("argument mapping for everyone") is dead or invisible. Every product that owned its Ring 1 wedge had the option to expand. Substack owned the writer wedge before adding business newsletters before adding video. Are.na owned the designer wedge before adding educators. Manifold owned the rationalist forecaster wedge before adding sports markets.

## Final summary

**Recommended primary audience:** Rationalist-adjacent / ACX-LW readers (a). Native vocabulary fit, concentrated and addressable, already rejecting the obvious competitor (Kialo). Spillover dynamics from (a) into (j) Substack writers and (c) journalists are favorable; reverse is not.

**Recommended secondary:** Substack writers / commentators (j). They're the bridge from wedge to mainstream and have medium-term WTP. EA and AI-safety audiences are natively included in the (a) wedge — no separate copy needed.

**Recommended homepage line:** "Maps the cruxes, evidence, and fallacies in any argument. For people who steelman before they argue." Vocabulary signals the wedge without naming a tribe; "steelman" and "crux" do the work.

**Three next-step actions:**

1. Rewrite the homepage hero and meta description to use wedge-native vocabulary (steelman, crux, update). Ship by end of week.
2. Write the /about page in the explicit voice — name ACX, LessWrong, EA Forum, Asterisk as the primary audience. Ship within 10 days.
3. Pick one analyzed topic with strong rationalist-adjacent salience (AI extinction-risk cruxes is the obvious choice given current discourse) and post it to ACX open thread + LessWrong + r/slatestarcodex with a deliberately tribe-native one-liner. Single test of the wedge before any further surface changes.
