# Brand Voice Architecture: The Editorial Masthead Model

**Date:** 2026-04-29
**Analyst:** Research agent 03/10, brand-voice architecture swarm
**Topic:** How a small named editorial board / contributor list creates a face for a publication without the founder being THE face — case studies and a recruitment plan for Argumend

## 1. The Masthead-as-Face Thesis

A masthead distributes the public-presence burden across multiple named individuals so the institution has a face — several, in fact — without any single person becoming THE face. A podcast booker looking up Foreign Affairs does not see "the Daniel Kurtz-Phelan magazine." They see Foreign Affairs, and Kurtz-Phelan happens to be its current editor. If he leaves, the institution does not.

Argumend's founder has two structural options for public presence: (a) become the founder-face — Yascha Mounk on Persuasion, Pitsos on Kialo — and pay for it with attack-vector exposure plus 20+ hr/week of personal-brand work; or (b) build a masthead, where 5-15 named editors, advisors, and contributors collectively absorb the "who is behind this?" question. Asterisk, Foreign Affairs, The New Atlantis, Persuasion, Compact, and City Journal have all chosen (b). None are anonymous; all have a face; none have *one* face. The masthead is also a routing table for inbound press: "we can connect you with our senior editor on AI policy" is bookable; "the founder doesn't do interviews" is not.

## 2. Six Case Studies

### Asterisk Magazine

Asterisk is the closest spiritual neighbor to Argumend in the masthead taxonomy: rationalist-adjacent, Open-Philanthropy-funded, quarterly print-and-digital, launched 2022. The masthead is **deliberately tiny on staff and enormous on contributors**.

**Core staff (4 named):**
- Clara Collier — Editor in Chief
- Angela Chen — Managing Editor
- Sarah Gephart / MGMT — Design
- Daniel G. Siegel — Web

**Contributors:** Approximately **195 named contributors** in the alphabetical contributors index ([asteriskmag.com/contributors](https://asteriskmag.com/contributors)), including Agnes Callard, Ajeya Cotra, Arvind Narayanan, Kelsey Piper, Matt Clancy, Zvi Mowshowitz, Adrian Tchaikovsky, Robin Wordsworth, Justin Sandefur, and so on.

**How PR is shared:** Collier is the single named representative for the publication — she does the podcasts (Patrick McKenzie's *Complex Systems*, EA Forum AMAs, etc.) and is the public quote when journalists need one ([Complex Systems podcast](https://www.complexsystemspodcast.com/episodes/building-institutions-that-bend-towards-truth-with-clara-collier-of-asterisk-magazine/)). But contributing writers carry their *own* pieces — Ajeya Cotra goes on AI-safety podcasts to talk about her Asterisk essay; Asterisk gets the byline credit. **Distributed PR via distributed authorship.**

**How new contributors join:** open pitching. The contributors page is essentially a record of who has pitched a piece that ran. There is no formal advisory board; instead, the contributor roster *is* the public-facing community.

**Lesson for Argumend:** A 4-person staff plus a long alphabetical contributors list is enough to look like an institution. You do not need a Council on Foreign Relations parent.

### Foreign Affairs

The opposite end of the spectrum: institutional weight first, individuals second. Founded 1922, bi-monthly, published by the **Council on Foreign Relations** — the institutional parent absorbs almost all the legitimacy work.

**Core editorial staff** ([foreignaffairs.com/staff](https://www.foreignaffairs.com/staff)):
- Daniel Kurtz-Phelan — Editor (since January 2021); holds the Peter G. Peterson Chair at the Council on Foreign Relations ([CFR profile](https://www.cfr.org/expert/daniel-kurtz-phelan))
- Justin Vogt — Executive Editor (joined 2011)
- Stuart A. Reid — Senior Editor
- Plus deputy/managing/copy editors and a books editor

**How PR is shared:** Kurtz-Phelan handles marquee interviews and keynotes; senior editors take issue-specific press. The CFR brand does the rest. When a Foreign Affairs piece goes viral, journalists call the *author* — usually figures like Fareed Zakaria or Anne-Marie Slaughter, whose own brands amplify FA's. The editor is a referee, not a star. New editors come in via internal promotion: Kurtz-Phelan was Executive Editor for three years first.

**Lesson for Argumend:** If Argumend ever sits inside an institutional parent (Open Philanthropy, Mercatus, Stanford HAI, or a future Argumend Foundation), the parent does the legitimacy work and the masthead just edits. Over-engineered for now; flag as long-arc destination.

### The New Atlantis

A quarterly journal of technology and society, founded 2003, originally inside the Ethics and Public Policy Center, became independent and is now published by the **Center for the Study of Technology and Society** — its own 501(c)(3) parent ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_Atlantis_(journal)), [EPPC announcement](https://eppc.org/news/the-new-atlantis-becomes-independent/)).

**Full masthead** ([thenewatlantis.com/about](https://www.thenewatlantis.com/about)):
- Ari Schulman — Editor
- Samuel Matlack — Managing Editor
- Louise Liebeskind — Assistant Editor
- Eric Cohen — Editor-at-Large (co-founder)
- Brady Lee — Director of Development & Communications
- **Senior Editors (5):** Brendan Foht, Adam Keiper, Caitrin Keiper, Yuval Levin, Christine Rosen
- **Contributing Editors (12):** James C. Capretta, Matthew B. Crawford, Alan Jacobs, Wilfred M. McClay, Gilbert Meilaender, Charles T. Rubin, Diana Schaub, Stephen L. Talbott, Raymond Tallis, Algis Valiunas, Adam J. White, Robert Zubrin
- Researcher: Melynda Boyce

**Total named:** ~22 people. This is the **gold standard small-publication masthead** — small enough to fit on a single page, large enough that no one person carries the brand.

**How PR is shared:** Schulman does the editor-of-record press; the senior editors and contributing editors do podcasts and interviews on their *individual* areas of expertise (Yuval Levin on policy, Matthew Crawford on craft and attention, Alan Jacobs on the humanities). The masthead is functionally a **distributed expertise network** — call the right name for the right topic.

**How new editors join:** elevation from contributing editor → senior editor → editor over years. Adam Keiper, a co-founder, stepped back from the lead editor role and stayed as Senior Editor. **The masthead survives founder transition** — exactly the property Argumend wants.

**Lesson for Argumend:** The New Atlantis is the template to copy almost beat-for-beat. Small staff, named senior editors, longer contributing-editors bench. ~22 names total.

### Persuasion

Yascha Mounk's Substack-published online magazine, founded July 2020, ~100K subscribers as of April 2025 ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasion_(online_magazine))). The cautionary tale: Persuasion has a board, but it is unmistakably **Mounk's project**.

- Yascha Mounk — Founder & Editor-in-Chief; weekly column; hosts *The Good Fight* podcast
- Francis Fukuyama — Chairs the American Purpose umbrella project
- **Board of Advisors / regulars:** Steven Pinker, Jonathan Haidt, Sheri Berman, Emily Yoffe, Olivia Nuzzi, Moisés Naím, Thomas Chatterton-Williams, Garry Kasparov, David French, Elif Shafak ([About](https://www.persuasion.community/about)).

**How PR is shared:** It isn't, really. Mounk is the brand. The board lends names to the website but the podcast, the column, and the public face are him. When a New Republic hit piece dropped, it was titled "The Problem With *Yascha Mounk's Persuasion*" ([TNR](https://newrepublic.com/article/158401/problem-yascha-mounks-persuasion)) — proving the structural point. Even a published advisory board cannot defend against founder-as-face if the founder is doing all the public work.

**Lesson for Argumend (negative):** A masthead alone does not protect you. If the founder is the only one on podcasts, writing the column, hosting the show, the advisors are decorative. **Persuasion is masthead-without-distribution.** Avoid this by making named senior editors actually do public work.

### Compact

Founded 2022 as a heterodox left-right "common good" magazine. The founding-editor structure is interesting because **the masthead survived a founder departure**.

**Founding editors:** Sohrab Ahmari, Edwin Aponte, Matthew Schmitz.

**Current masthead ([compactmag.com/about](https://www.compactmag.com/about/)):**
- Matthew Schmitz — Editor
- Geoff Shullenberger — Managing Editor
- Ashley Frawley — Senior Editor
- Gregory Conti — Editor-at-Large
- Stephen G. Adubato — Associate Editor

**Staff columnists** (named regular contributors): Ashley Frawley, Geoff Shullenberger, Gregory Conti, Matthew Schmitz, plus rotating writers Thomas Fazi, Julia Yost, Christopher Caldwell, Julie Bindel, John B. Judis.

**How PR is shared:** Each editor has a topic lane (Frawley on culture, Shullenberger on tech, Conti on the left, Schmitz on religion/right) and does podcasts in that lane. Sohrab Ahmari, the most public of the three founders, exited the editorship and continued his personal public career — and Compact didn't collapse. **Proof that a small editorial team can outlive its loudest founder** if the editorial team has its own real bylines.

**Lesson for Argumend:** Stack the masthead with named editors who already write under their own bylines elsewhere. They bring their own audience; they don't need yours; you don't depend on any one of them.

### City Journal

Manhattan Institute's quarterly, founded 1990. The **biggest masthead of the six**, because of the institutional parent.

**Senior leadership** ([city-journal.org/about](https://www.city-journal.org/about)):
- Brian C. Anderson — Editor (since 2008)
- Steven Malanga — Senior Fellow & Senior Editor
- Paul Beston — Managing Editor
- Charles Fain Lehman — Senior Fellow & Senior Editor
- Plus 3 associate editors and 6 production staff
- **Contributing writers:** several hundred, alphabetical ([city-journal.org/contributors](https://www.city-journal.org/contributors))
- **Parent:** Manhattan Institute for Policy Research (EIN 13-2912529) — fundraising, governance, policy-think-tank legitimacy

**How PR is shared:** Anderson runs the publication; senior fellows like Lehman, Malanga, and Heather Mac Donald do Fox/cable/podcasts on their topic areas; Manhattan Institute development handles foundations. **Three-layer separation: institution, editor, fellows.**

**Lesson for Argumend:** Institutional parent + editor + named fellows is the most durable structure but over-built for pre-revenue. City Journal is what Argumend could grow *into* over 5-10 years.

## 3. The Founder-on-the-Masthead Question

Should Argumend's founder appear on the masthead at all?

**Pros of being listed (e.g., "Founder & Publisher"):** legitimacy (anonymous publications are read suspiciously); clear ownership of editorial standards (necessary for foundation grants and serious journalism partnerships); avoids the "why won't they put their name to it?" attack vector that founders create when they try to disappear entirely.

**Cons:** re-introduces the personal-attack surface; risks the Persuasion failure mode (name on masthead, doing all public work, masthead decorative); founder's name naturally accumulates SEO weight over time and becomes synonymous with the project.

**Recommendation: yes, list the founder, but as one of several names of equal visual weight.** Mounk's name on Persuasion fails not because it's on the masthead but because it *is* the masthead. Schulman on The New Atlantis works because he is one of 22 names with ~6 of similar credibility — Yuval Levin is more famous in policy circles, Matthew Crawford in cultural criticism. The founder gets diluted in plain sight.

**Concrete:** "Editor & Publisher: [Real Name]" — same font size as other editors, no portrait, short bio. The founder does *not* do the bulk of podcast work; that is delegated to topic-specialist senior editors as soon as the bench fills out. Pseudonym option exists but is structurally weaker; see the swarm's anonymous-vs-pseudonymous research for that branch.

## 4. Recruiting an Editorial Advisory Board for Argumend

**Target:** 5-7 part-time advisors, recruited weeks 4-12 of the post-freeze plan.

**Profile criteria:**
1. PhD-level or equivalent epistemics-curious thinker
2. Already publishes under their own byline somewhere (Substack, journal, blog)
3. Can spend 2-4 hours/month minimum on Argumend (review pieces, write occasional essay, do one podcast/year)
4. Has a public audience that does not depend on Argumend

**Candidate archetypes:**
- **Argument-mapping veterans:** Tim van Gelder (Melbourne, Rationale/bCisive), David Manley (Michigan), Simon Cullen (CMU, ThinkerAnalytix). One of these on the masthead does enormous credibility work — they have spent careers on the exact problem Argumend automates.
- **Debate-coach types:** Bo Seo (Harvard debate, *Good Arguments* author).
- **AI-safety-and-epistemics:** Ozzie Gooen (QURI, EA Forum) is the most obvious fit. Misha Yagudin or Eli Lifland (Samotsvety, AI Futures Project) for forecasting credibility.
- **Journalists adjacent to the territory:** Peter Wildeford, Kelsey Piper, Dylan Matthews.
- **Podcastable philosophers:** Liam Kofi Bright (LSE), Agnes Callard (Chicago), Will MacAskill.

**Compensation:** $0-5K/year stipend per advisor, paid quarterly. Optional small equity if Argumend ever becomes a benefit corporation; optional named credit on a future research grant. The serious money signal is not the stipend — it is Argumend covering travel for one in-person editorial retreat per year ($2-3K all-in) and listing them prominently with portrait, bio, link to their personal site.

**Total annual budget:** $0-35K, realistically $10-15K for a 5-person board with one $2K/person stipend plus a retreat.

**Outreach script (cold email template):**

> Subject: Argumend editorial board invitation
>
> [Name],
>
> I'm building Argumend (argumend.org) — an AI-augmented argument-mapping platform that visualizes controversial topics as interactive graphs of positions, evidence, cruxes, and fallacies. We have ~110 pre-analyzed topics and a multi-model judge council for live debates.
>
> I'm assembling a small editorial advisory board (5-7 people) of thinkers whose work on epistemics, argumentation, or forecasting I've followed. Your work on [specific thing] is exactly the kind of public-facing rigor I want this project to be associated with.
>
> The ask: 2-4 hours/month — review one essay or topic map, weigh in on editorial direction, do one podcast or guest essay per year. Compensation is a $2K/year stipend, named position on the masthead with byline credit, and an annual editorial retreat (travel covered).
>
> Happy to send a one-page brief and a 15-min Loom of the platform if you want to see it before deciding. Either way, would love your reaction.
>
> [Founder, Argumend]

**Targeting cadence:** 8-12 outreach emails to land 5 yeses. Personalize each. Reference at least one specific piece of their writing in the second sentence. Do not send a mass mailmerge — this is reputation infrastructure, not lead-gen.

## 5. The Contributor-Author Byline Pattern

Even before a formal advisory board exists, Argumend can populate its blog with **named contributor bylines** — real or pseudonymous — to expand the visible "we" without salaried staff. Mechanics: cross-post from EA Forum, LessWrong, and Substack with author permission (EA Forum's CC norm makes this trivial); pay $200-500 per commissioned essay; curate to topic areas (existential risk, AI policy, public health, philosophy of science); pseudonymous bylines OK if identity is known to editor (matches Asterisk's "Dynomight" treatment). Targets: Ozzie Gooen, Zvi Mowshowitz, Dynomight, Astera Institute, Roots of Progress writers.

**Visible payoff:** an authors index with 20-30 named bylines after 6 months of light commissioning, at ~$5-10K. This *looks* like an institution to a journalist Googling "who writes for Argumend."

## 6. The "Editor Character" Pattern

The Economist runs columns under fixed pseudonyms — Bagehot (UK), Charlemagne (EU), Lexington (US), Banyan (Asia), Chaguan (China). The columnist behind each is anonymous and rotates over years; the pseudonym persists. Economist editors explicitly say "collective voice and personality matter more than the identities of individual journalists" ([Wikipedia: The Economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist)). Walter Bagehot the man died in 1877; Bagehot the column will outlive whoever currently writes it.

**Argumend application:** "Cruxtacean's Notes" — a monthly column written under the institutional pseudonym of the existing Cruxtacean agent persona (already a code-level construct in `lib/agents/`).

**Why this works:** the Cruxtacean character is already in the product vocabulary; promoting it from product feature to institutional voice is natural. The column can be written by the founder, a contributor, or AI-with-human-editing — readers follow the persona, not the human. Pseudonymous columns have a literary defense (Bagehot, Cato, Publius); not "hiding" but "writing in a voice." Removes the "who is this?" attack surface from the column.

**Risk:** if it gets good enough that journalists *want* to know who Cruxtacean is, the pseudonym becomes a story. The Economist accepts this; columnists are doxxable but not doxxed by the publication. Argumend's policy: never confirm or deny.

## 7. Costs and Effort

**Founder time, masthead model:** ~5-10 hr/month — editing/scheduling contributor pieces (4-6h), board management (2-3h), publishing pipeline (1-2h).

**Founder time, founder-face model:** ~36-54 hr/month, or 10-20 hr/week — personal-brand X presence (12-20h), podcasts + prep (6-12h), personal essays (8-12h), conference presence (~10h).

**Cash cost:** masthead $10-35K/year (5 stipends + commissions + retreat); founder-face $0 direct, but 10-20 hr/week of founder attention diverted from product is easily $50-150K-equivalent in shadow cost.

**Verdict:** the masthead is meaningfully cheaper in founder hours and only somewhat more expensive in cash. The cash is also *legible* — stipends and retreats are grant-line-itemable. Founder time is not.

## 8. Recommended Argumend Masthead Architecture

Concrete, shippable structure:

**Tier 1 — Editor & Publisher (1 person):**
Founder, real name OR pseudonym (separate decision, see anonymous-brand-voice swarm). One named individual responsible for editorial standards. Bio: 3 sentences. Portrait: optional. Public posture: minimal; deflect podcasts to senior editors as the bench fills out.

**Tier 2 — Editorial Board (3-5 named advisors, recruited weeks 4-12 post-freeze):**
PhD-level or equivalent thinkers with their own audiences. Each gets a topic lane (epistemics, argument mapping, forecasting, AI safety, philosophy). Each takes 1-2 podcast bookings per year on Argumend's behalf. Compensation: $2K/year + retreat. **Target by mid-2026: Tim van Gelder or David Manley (argument mapping), Ozzie Gooen (forecasting/epistemics), Bo Seo or equivalent (debate pedagogy), one journalist (Kelsey Piper / Dylan Matthews tier), one philosopher.**

**Tier 3 — Contributors (rotating, 20-50 named guest writers over the first year):**
Cross-posts and commissioned essays. $200-500 per piece. Names appear on individual posts with full bylines. No retainer, no commitment past the piece. Cumulative effect: an "authors" page with ~30 names after 6 months.

**Tier 4 — Cruxtacean (institutional pseudonym):**
A blog column, monthly. Persona-voiced, written by founder + contributors + AI in some mix. Never doxxed by Argumend. The Bagehot of the project.

**Visible result on argumend.org/about:**
- 1 publisher
- 5 advisors with portraits
- 30 contributors in an alphabetical index
- 1 named pseudonymous columnist

That is **~37 visible "people"** behind Argumend, with no single person doing more than ~10 hours/week of public-facing work. This is the architecture that makes Argumend look like an institution while the founder remains backstage doing product.

---

## Final summary

**Recommended masthead:** Editor & Publisher (founder, one of many names), Editorial Board of 3-5 part-time named advisors recruited from the argument-mapping / forecasting / epistemics world, ~30 rotating contributors gathered via cross-post + light commissioning, and one institutional pseudonymous column ("Cruxtacean's Notes"). Closest reference template: The New Atlantis (~22 names, no founder-face). Avoid the Persuasion failure mode — masthead alone is decorative if the founder still does all public work; named senior editors must actually carry podcast and press duties in their lanes.

**Three next-step actions:**
1. **Weeks 4-6:** Draft the four-tier About page wireframe and ship a placeholder masthead with founder + 1-2 already-aligned contributors named. Do not wait for the full board.
2. **Weeks 6-12:** Send 8-12 personalized outreach emails to the candidate advisors (Tim van Gelder, Ozzie Gooen, Bo Seo, Kelsey Piper, one philosopher). Budget $10K/year for 5 stipends + retreat. Track yes/no/maybe in a single spreadsheet.
3. **Week 8:** Launch "Cruxtacean's Notes" as the first institutional pseudonymous column — first three posts already drafted before launch so cadence is credible. Cross-post first one to LessWrong and EA Forum to seed the byline-and-pseudonym pattern publicly.
