# Current Controversy: Solar Geoengineering — SAI, MCB, and the Unilateral-Deployment Debate

**Date:** 2026-04-28
**Analyst:** Research sweep for Argumend current-controversies dispatch (07/10)
**Topic scope:** Stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI), marine cloud brightening (MCB), governance regimes, and the unilateral-deployment debate as of April 2026.

## 1. Snapshot — geoengineering state April 2026

The solar geoengineering debate in April 2026 is at an awkward inflection: research is gathering institutional momentum even as the most visible deployment-adjacent experiments keep dying on the table.

**Harvard SCoPEx is dead.** The Stratospheric Controlled Perturbation Experiment, the totemic field experiment that defined "responsible solar geoengineering research" for a decade under David Keith and Frank Keutsch, was formally cancelled in **March 2024** ([Harvard Crimson](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2024/3/20/harvard-geoengineering-project-abandoned/), [MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2024/04/04/1090626/the-hard-lessons-of-harvards-failed-geoengineering-experiment/)). The Saami Council's 2021 letter halting the Kiruna test flight was the inflection point; Keutsch publicly suspended work in August 2023, and Keith decamped to the **University of Chicago Climate Systems Engineering initiative**. Harvard's Solar Geoengineering Research Program continues at the **Salata Institute**, but the platform is being repurposed for non-SRM stratospheric science ([Salata Institute update](https://salatainstitute.harvard.edu/an-update-on-scopex/)).

**The Alameda CAARE/CARI marine cloud brightening trial was killed in June 2024**, when the Alameda City Council voted unanimously to evict the University of Washington team from the USS Hornet flight deck, despite city-commissioned experts finding "little to no risk" ([SRM360](https://srm360.org/news-reaction/details-emerge-cancelled-experiment-alameda/), [CIEL](https://www.ciel.org/news/ciel-welcomes-alamedas-no-to-geoengineering-experiment/)). Researchers said they were exploring alternative sites; as of April 2026 a public follow-up experiment in US territory has not materialised.

**Make Sunsets is alive and embattled.** Luke Iseman and Andrew Song's two-person Boost VC / Pioneer Fund-backed startup has now launched 120+ balloons of SO₂ from US soil, banned in Mexico since January 2023, and has been served a Section 114 Clean Air Act information request by EPA's Office of Air and Radiation in April 2025 ([Inside Climate News](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/18042025/epa-targets-sulfur-dioxide-balloons-startup/), [EPA](https://www.epa.gov/so2-pollution/epa-seeks-information-make-sunsets)). They sell "cooling credits" at $5–30 a pop. As of mid-2025 they had released roughly 0.1 tons of SO₂. Symbolic vs. atmospheric — but the **regulatory precedent** matters more than the payload.

**State-level US bans cascading.** **Tennessee** became the first state to pass a chemtrails-flavoured ban (signed by Gov. Bill Lee, effective July 1, 2024). **Florida SB-56** passed the Senate 28-9 in April 2025, signed by DeSantis, effective July 1, 2025, with third-degree felony penalties up to $100,000 for unauthorised "injection, release, or dispersion" ([Florida House Analysis](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2025/477/Analyses/h0477z1.NRD.PDF), [SRM360 tracker](https://srm360.org/news-reaction/us-states-consider-bills-to-ban-geoengineering/)). Conspiracy-coded politics is now the dominant US deployment risk vector — not Make Sunsets.

**UNEA-6 (Feb 2024) failed to produce a resolution.** Switzerland's draft to convene a UN Expert Scientific Group on SRM was withdrawn after African states, joined by Colombia, Fiji, Mexico, Pakistan and Vanuatu, demanded any text acknowledge the existing 2010 CBD moratorium and the Non-Use Agreement push ([Climate Change News](https://www.climatechangenews.com/2024/02/29/nations-fail-to-agree-ban-or-research-on-solar-geoengineering-regulations/), [CIEL](https://www.ciel.org/news/ciel-response-to-geoengineering-srm-technology-rejection-unea-6/)). At **UNEA-7 (December 2025)** the Africa Group launched a sidelines plurilateral dialogue on a Solar Geoengineering Non-Use Agreement ([CIEL on UNEA-7](https://www.ciel.org/news/unea-7-conclusion/)). The **AMCEN ministerial meeting in July 2025** "unequivocally rejected" SRM as having any role in climate response ([Solar Geoengineering Non-Use Agreement](https://www.solargeoeng.org/)).

**The Non-Use Agreement letter (Biermann et al., 2022)** has crossed **600+ academic signatories from 65 countries** and 2,000+ civil society organisations as of January 2026 ([Non-Use Agreement signatories](https://www.solargeoeng.org/non-use-agreement/signatories/)). The **EU Group of Chief Scientific Advisors** in December 2024 advised the European Commission to push for a global moratorium ([Climate Change News](https://www.climatechangenews.com/2024/12/09/eu-should-push-for-global-deal-not-to-deploy-solar-geoengineering-advisors-say/)).

**Pro-research infrastructure is consolidating.** **SilverLining** (the US NGO led by Kelly Wanser) has distributed $3M+ in research grants to UW, Cornell, Rutgers, NCAR. The **Degrees Initiative** (formerly SRMGI, run by **Andy Parker** out of Bristol) ran the largest-ever SRM conference in Cape Town in May 2025, explicitly Global-South-centred. The **Solar Geoengineering Research Governance (SGRG) Platform** — a US/UK/India coalition — was announced February 2026 with a formal launch in July 2026 ([SolarQuarter](https://solarquarter.com/2026/02/26/global-coalition-launches-solar-geoengineering-research-governance-platform-to-strengthen-transparency-and-accountability/)). **Wake Smith** (Yale) co-authored an April 2025 paper showing existing aircraft could deliver 12 Mt SO₂/yr at 13km over poles for ~0.6°C cooling ([UCL News](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/news/2025/apr/geoengineering-technique-could-cool-planet-using-existing-aircraft), [Earth's Future](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2025/04/250428221708.htm)) — and a 2025 *Global Policy* paper on US/China deployment pathways ([Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1758-5899.70015)).

**China and India research programs persist but underpublicised.** China's Beijing Normal University-anchored program (~$3M federal, ~15 faculty across three institutions) studies regional aerosol impacts on glaciers and the Asian monsoon. India's Department of Science and Technology funds Indian Institute of Science work on monsoon sensitivity. Neither government has signalled deployment intent; both maintain research independence ([RFF](https://www.rff.org/topics/comprehensive-climate-strategies/solar-geoengineering/)).

**Funders.** Open Philanthropy (now Coefficient Giving) has historically funded Harvard SGRP ($2.5M to Keith). SilverLining gets money from the Pritzker Innovation Fund, Moore Foundation, Grantham, Chris and Crystal Sacca. Survival and Flourishing Fund's 2025 round disbursed $34.92M with explicit "long-term survival" framing — geoengineering shows up adjacent to GCR/x-risk grant clusters ([SFF](https://survivalandflourishing.fund/recommendations)).

## 2. The four-camp positioning

### Camp A — Pro-research, pro-careful-deployment ("the prudent risk-managers")

**Voices:** David Keith (UChicago), Andy Parker (Degrees Initiative), Wake Smith (Yale), Kelly Wanser (SilverLining), Frank Keutsch (Harvard), the Climate Overshoot Commission majority (Pascal Lamy, chair), ClimateWorks Foundation, Reflecting Sunlight (NASEM 2021) authors.

**Core claim:** Climate damages are real and large, mitigation is lagging, overshoot is likely; SRM may be the only available tool to limit peak-warming damage; therefore society must develop the science *now*, under transparent governance, so that any future deployment decision is informed rather than panicked. Moral hazard is real but manageable; not researching is itself a form of moral hazard ([Issues in Science and Tech](https://issues.org/geoengineering-solar-intervention-climate-moral-hazard/)).

**Tells:** "Risk-risk framing." Insistence that SRM is a *complement* not a substitute. NASEM 2021's "transdisciplinary research program... not a substitute for reducing emissions" framing.

### Camp B — Anti-deployment, restrict-research ("the precautionaries")

**Voices:** Frank Biermann (Utrecht), Aarti Gupta (Wageningen), the Saami Council, ETC Group (Silvia Ribeiro), Hands Off Mother Earth (HOME) Alliance, CIEL (Center for International Environmental Law), Geoengineering Monitor, Climate Vulnerable Forum, AMCEN (African Ministerial Conference on the Environment).

**Core claim:** SRM cannot be governed by any plausible international institution; even research normalises deployment ("slippery slope"); side-effects on monsoons and ozone fall on the global South; indigenous and Southern peoples have not consented; therefore prohibit research, deployment, patents, and inclusion in any policy framework. Five demands of the Non-Use Agreement: no deployment, no public funding, no outdoor experiments, no patents, no inclusion in international institutions ([WIREs](https://wires.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/wcc.754)).

**Tells:** "Speculative technology." Frame geoengineering as fossil-fuel-industry distraction. Cite the 2010 CBD moratorium as already-binding.

### Camp C — Climate-realist / overshoot pragmatists ("the inevitabilists")

**Voices:** Holly Buck (Buffalo), Daniel Bodansky (ASU law), Michael Gerrard (Columbia), Jesse Reynolds, Joshua Horton, the Climate Overshoot Commission's overshoot-is-likely framing.

**Core claim:** 1.5°C is gone, 2°C probably gone, overshoot is the path we're on; some actor *will* eventually deploy SRM unilaterally if the heat damages get bad enough; the only choice is whether deployment happens informed-and-multilateral or panicked-and-unilateral; therefore build the science, the law, and the diplomatic infrastructure now, including for the worst case. Bodansky's 2025 RFF/Harvard workshop talk was titled "Why a global moratorium on solar geoengineering deployment should get a chilly reception" ([RFF event](https://www.rff.org/events/conferences/2025-rff-and-harvard-srm-social-science-research-workshop-governance-in-a-fractured-world/)).

**Tells:** "Avoiding the worst form of deployment." Buck's *After Geoengineering* (2019) frames the question as repair, not technofix. Treats unilateral deployment by a heat-stressed mid-power as the *base rate*, not an outlier.

### Camp D — Tech-optimist accelerationists ("just do it")

**Voices:** Make Sunsets / Luke Iseman / Andrew Song. Boost VC and Pioneer Fund. e/acc-coded climate Twitter (e.g. handles like @ClimateMcCain). Some Balaji-adjacent network-state climate framing.

**Core claim:** Climate is a real catastrophe, governance is impossibly slow, the cost-effectiveness math on SAI cooling credits versus carbon offsets is overwhelming, just deploy and let the regulators catch up. Sue later. The cooling credit is a genuine market product.

**Tells:** "Cooling credits" pricing in dollars per gram. Direct-to-consumer Stripe checkout for atmospheric intervention. Hostility to NASEM-style governance preconditions.

## 3. Top 5 cruxes

**Crux 1 — Moral hazard: does SRM availability slow mitigation effort?** Empirically mixed. Andrews et al. (2022) experimental geoengineering game found anticipating moral hazard *itself* undermines mitigation ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0921800922000830)). McEvoy et al. (2024) modelling shows substitution toward SRM grows as it more closely substitutes for mitigation in damage compensation. Counter-evidence: lab studies in collective-risk dilemmas suggest the *threat* of SRM can *increase* cooperation ([Issues in S&T](https://issues.org/geoengineering-solar-intervention-climate-moral-hazard/)). Real-world political evidence is non-existent because deployment hasn't happened. **Crux disposition: empirically open.**

**Crux 2 — Termination shock catastrophe.** Parker & Irvine (2018) *Earth's Future* showed termination shock is real but policy-mitigable: distributed deployment infrastructure, redundant launch sites, treaty obligations and stockpiles can all blunt it ([Earth's Future](https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/2017EF000735)). Critics counter that *any* such system is a hostage-to-fortune that locks civilisation into perpetual aerosol injection. **Camp B treats termination shock as an axiomatic deal-breaker; Camp A treats it as a research target.**

**Crux 3 — Governance feasibility.** Can any global institution credibly constrain a single state (or actor) from unilateral deployment? Bodansky's 2013 governance taxonomy and 2025 chilly-reception talk argue formal moratoria are unstable because they don't bind the actor most likely to deploy. Biermann argues a non-use norm can work the way the chemical weapons norm works — through legitimacy, not enforcement. **This is the deepest crux because it is partly empirical (institutional capacity) and partly performative (norms work if people believe in them).**

**Crux 4 — Regional side effects, especially monsoons and ozone.** Multiple modelling studies show 5–10% Indian Summer Monsoon precipitation reduction under G4-style SAI scenarios, with risks of monsoon-switch-off under uneven deployment ([Hussain 2025 *J. Climatology*](https://rmets.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/joc.8695), [Krishnamohan & Bala 2021 *Sci. Reports*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-89249-6), [npj CAS 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41612-024-00875-z)). 2025 work on the seven homogeneous monsoon rainfall regions reaffirms reductions across South Asia ([Aerosol Sci. Eng. 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41810-025-00341-0)). **Camp A: "model uncertainty bounds, manageable with optimisation." Camp B: "1.6 billion people downstream of your tuning parameter."**

**Crux 5 — Justice and consent.** Who decides? The Saami Council's 2021 letter and AMCEN's 2025 declaration both ground their objections in non-consent, not technical risk ([ETC Group on Saami](https://www.etcgroup.org/content/saami-swedes-and-civil-society-stop-solar-geoengineering-trial-balloon)). Camp A responds that *not* deploying is also a decision imposed on heat-vulnerable populations who would benefit from cooling. **Both sides claim to speak for the global South; the global South itself is not unanimous (small island states sometimes diverge from AMCEN).**

## 5. Fallacies and rhetorical moves

- **Naturalistic fallacy** — "natural climate good, modified climate bad." Common in HOME/ETC framing. Ignores that the current climate is itself heavily modified by humans; the question is which modification.
- **Slippery slope** — "research inevitably leads to deployment." Camp B's load-bearing claim. Empirically contestable: SCoPEx and Alameda were both *halted by* research-stage opposition, suggesting the slope grips. But Make Sunsets shows the slope can be jumped without the research stage at all.
- **False dichotomy** — "cut emissions OR geoengineer." Both NASEM and Overshoot Commission explicitly reject this; the field's mainstream framing is *complement*, not substitute. The dichotomy survives in advocacy on both sides.
- **Appeal to consequences (catastrophe-flavour)** — "termination shock would kill billions" used as ipso facto reason to ban research, which isn't responsive to the Parker-Irvine claim that termination shock is policy-mitigable.
- **Appeal to consequences (TINA-flavour)** — "we're going to overshoot, so SRM is inevitable, therefore start now." The Camp C move, structurally identical to the previous one but with opposite political valence.
- **Genetic fallacy / poisoning the well** — "fossil fuel funded" allegations in HOME/ETC framing aimed at researchers who in fact have no fossil-fuel funding. Conversely, dismissing Camp B as "anti-science" elides their actual sophisticated empirical claims about monsoons and governance.
- **Equivocation on "geoengineering"** — chemtrails-conspiracy bills like Florida SB-56 conflate cloud-seeding (decades old, mundane), MCB (early research), SAI (no deployment), and outright fictional government weather control. Argumend should disambiguate aggressively.

## 7. Argumend-fit score

**9/10 — high-fit topic.**

This is close to ideal Argumend material. It is:
- **Technical** — the cruxes resolve to empirical questions (termination-shock dynamics, monsoon model spread, moral-hazard substitution elasticity) that *can* be argued with evidence.
- **Multi-camp** — four genuine positions, not two; the four-way structure is exactly what Argumend's graph format reveals better than pro/con trees.
- **Rationalist-coded** — EA Forum, 80,000 Hours, LessWrong have all engaged. The risk-risk framing is native to the rationalist toolkit.
- **Open Philanthropy adjacent** — Coefficient Giving's history of funding Harvard SGRP and the Sacca/Pritzker/Moore network gives Argumend a credible distribution channel into Camp A.
- **Newsworthy** — Florida SB-56, the EPA-Make Sunsets dance, AMCEN, UNEA-7 sidelines all give the topic ongoing news pegs.

The 1-point dock is for **emotional radioactivity** (see §8) — this topic will draw vitriol from Camp B if Argumend's framing reads as pro-tech.

## 8. Risk and sensitivity flags

- **Environmental-left allergy.** The mainstream environmental movement (Greenpeace, Friends of the Earth, Sierra Club-adjacent) treats geoengineering as a fossil-industry distraction tactic. Any Argumend graph that *appears* to balance Camp A and Camp B as equally credible risks immediate "false balance" / "platforming denialism" backlash.
- **"Anti-science" trap.** Camp A advocates sometimes paint Camp B as anti-science. This is wrong — Biermann, Gupta, and the AMCEN scientists are not flat-earthers, they are governance theorists with empirical claims. Argumend must not accept this framing.
- **Indigenous sovereignty / Global South consent.** The Saami Council's veto over SCoPEx and AMCEN's collective rejection are *consent* claims, not technical claims. Argumend should foreground these as a distinct *node type* (governance consent) rather than dismissing them as ordinary policy preferences.
- **Chemtrails contamination.** The Florida/Tennessee bans conflate scientific SRM with conspiracy chemtrails. Argumend should aggressively disambiguate to avoid the topic getting pulled into RFK-coded conspiracy aesthetics. This is a real and growing reputational hazard for the entire field — and for any platform that maps it.
- **Make Sunsets framing.** It is easy to make Iseman & Song the avatar of geoengineering and dunk on them. This is journalistically tempting, intellectually lazy, and hands the precautionary camp a strawman. Argumend should locate Make Sunsets as Camp D — a real but tiny corner — not the centre.
- **Funder optics.** Argumend is Coefficient-Giving / SFF-network adjacent in vibe. If those names appear too prominently in the graph, the platform looks like Camp A advocacy. Surface them honestly but don't let them dominate the source list.

The right Argumend posture is **governance-complexity-forward, not pro-tech**: foreground the consent-and-institutions cruxes, treat the technical cruxes as genuinely open, and let the four camps argue without the platform thumb-on-scale. The richer node taxonomy (positions / evidence / cruxes / fallacies / meta) is the structural advantage here — the pro/con tree literally cannot represent the four-camp structure without distortion.

## Summary

The most-contested crux is **governance feasibility** — every other crux (moral hazard, termination shock, monsoon side-effects, justice) ultimately routes through "can any institution credibly bind the actor most likely to deploy?" Camps A, B, and C disagree fundamentally on this and the disagreement is *both* empirical and performative.

**Argumend-fit verdict: 9/10, ship.** This is the rare topic where the four-camp structure plus rationalist-coded technical cruxes plus live policy news pegs all line up. Risk vector is reputational (false-balance allegations from environmental left; chemtrails contamination from the right) — mitigate by foregrounding the governance and consent cruxes rather than the pro-research narrative.

**Three voices to cite first**, one per major axis: **Holly Buck** (overshoot pragmatist, *After Geoengineering*) for the inevitability framing; **Frank Biermann** (Utrecht / Non-Use Agreement) for the precautionary/governance-impossible position; **Wake Smith** (Yale, 2025 polar-SAI paper) for the technical-feasibility evidence. David Keith and Andy Parker can come in the second pass.
