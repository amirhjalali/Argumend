The regulatory landscape moved a lot in the last six months:

- **EU AI Act:** Chapter V GPAI obligations have been in force since August 2, 2025; **enforcement powers** activate **August 2, 2026**, the same date the high-risk system regime goes operational. Open-source / open-weight providers under truly free licenses get a partial exemption — they only have to comply with the copyright policy and training-content summary provisions ([EU AI Act enforcement](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/enforcement-of-chapter-v-under-the-eu-ai-act/), [Pearl Cohen](https://www.pearlcohen.com/new-guidance-under-the-eu-ai-act-ahead-of-its-next-enforcement-date/)).

No kill-switch mandate, no testing regime ([Fisher Phillips](https://www.fisherphillips.com/en/insights/insights/california-lawmakers-pass-landmark-ai-transparency-law-for-frontier-models), [FPF](https://fpf.org/blog/californias-sb-53-the-first-frontier-ai-law-explained/)).

## 3. Top 5 cruxes

### Crux 1 — Capability ceiling: can open-weight models actually reach dangerous capabilities?

**The disagreement.** Open-weight pros (LeCun, Ng) point out that current open-weight CBRN evaluations show no qualitative uplift over Google search; safety camp (Bengio, Hendrycks) point to the trajectory. Concretely: as of April 2026, DeepSeek V4-Pro is at 3,206 Codeforces and ~80% on SWE-Bench Verified — that *is* approaching superhuman cybercoding capability. Cybench (40 professional CTF tasks, [OpenReview](https://openreview.net/forum?id=tc90LV0yRL)) and RE-Bench (ML research engineering, [METR](https://metr.org/blog/2024-11-22-evaluating-r-d-capabilities-of-llms/)) are the live measurement tools.

**Where the data lives.** Epoch AI's analysis ([Epoch](https://epoch.ai/gradient-updates/do-the-biorisk-evaluations-of-ai-labs-actually-measure-the-risk-of-developing-bioweapons)) says current biorisk evals "do not provide strong evidence that LLMs can enable amateurs to develop bioweapons." But the same author concedes the evals are weak signals.

### Crux 2 — Marginal harm: do open weights enable NEW capability or duplicate closed-model capability?

The strongest empirical claim from the safety camp is the **elicitation attack** literature — see the LessWrong "AI Safety at the Frontier" January 2026 paper highlights ([LW](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/JcAm6MFog6ssKooFN/ai-safety-at-the-frontier-paper-highlights-of-january-2026)), which describes attackers using safeguarded frontier systems to uplift unrestricted open-weight models on dangerous tasks without ever directly requesting harmful information. This collapses the "but the closed model already knows it" argument into "the closed model knows it but won't say it; the open model can be made to say it." Pros (a16z, LeCun) counter that this proves the bottleneck is RLHF safety training, not pretraining knowledge — which open weights merely expose. The *marginal* harm is a function of how reliably closed models stay closed.

### Crux 3 — China geopolitics: does restricting US open weights cede the field?

This is the crux that flipped the Trump administration. The July 2025 AI Action Plan ([PDF](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Americas-AI-Action-Plan.pdf)) explicitly endorsed open-weight models as a strategic asset *because* of Chinese competition. Stanford HAI's "Beyond DeepSeek: China's Diverse Open-Weight AI Ecosystem" ([HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/policy/beyond-deepseek-chinas-diverse-open-weight-ai-ecosystem-and-its-policy-implications)) and Bloomberg's April 27 piece "Why China's DeepSeek, Qwen and Moonshot Are a Worry for US AI Rivals" ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/why-china-s-deepseek-qwen-and-moonshot-are-a-worry-for-us-ai-rivals)) both make the affirmative case. Bengio's counterargument: Chinese open weights don't make US open weights *safer*; they make the global open-weight equilibrium *more dangerous*.

### Crux 4 — Liability framework: who owes the duty of care?

The EU's Product Liability Directive treats substantial fine-tuners as "manufacturers" — meaning the downstream actor, not the original releaser, bears liability ([Federated compliance paper](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212473X25001063)). This is the open-weight pro's preferred regime: Meta releases Llama 4 with reasonable safeguards, fine-tuner X strips them, fine-tuner X is liable. The safety camp's counter: open weights are "irreversible" (Crux 5), so liability after the fact is post-hoc accounting on a permanent harm.

### Crux 5 — Reversibility: can open weights be "recalled"?

This is the technically settled crux. They cannot. NTIA's policy-approaches report ([NTIA](https://www.ntia.gov/programs-and-initiatives/artificial-intelligence/open-model-weights-report/policy-approaches-recommendations/policy-approaches)) acknowledges this; the "Tamper-Resistant Safeguards for Open-Weight LLMs" paper ([arXiv 2408.00761](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2408.00761)) and "On Evaluating the Durability of Safeguards" ([arXiv 2412.07097](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.07097)) show that current safety fine-tuning for open weights can be stripped with "a few hundred euros" of toxic-example fine-tuning. Where camps disagree is the *implication*: restrictionists say "irreversibility means extreme caution at release"; pros say "irreversibility means model-level controls are the wrong intervention; regulate use, not release."

## 5. Fallacies and rhetorical moves

1. **Appeal to authority (Hinton/Bengio).** Both sides do this, but the safety camp leans on it most. "Two of the three Turing-Award godfathers say X" is heavy in op-eds. LeCun (the third godfather, of course) is regularly invoked symmetrically by the open-weight camp. The argument from sheer credentialed alarm doesn't tell you what the marginal-harm calculation is — it tells you the priors of three computer scientists.
2. **Slippery slope ("if we ban Llama 5, we ban Llama 6, eventually we ban PyTorch").** a16z and Ng deploy this — sometimes correctly (regulatory capture is a real failure mode) and sometimes as a thought-stopper.
3. **False dichotomy ("ban or release").** The actual policy menu — staged release, structured access, downloadable-but-export-controlled, capability-gated release, post-deployment liability — is a continuum the public debate flattens.
4. **Strawman ("doomers want to ban math").** A common e/acc move; in practice no serious safety researcher advocates banning research. Symmetric strawman from the safety side: "open-weight pros want zero regulation."
5. **Motte-and-bailey on "AI safety means doomerism."** The motte: "powerful systems should be evaluated before deployment." The bailey: "any sufficiently capable model is an extinction risk." The first is unobjectionable; the second is contested. The terms get conflated to discredit the first via association with the second — and conversely, weakened to defend the second by retreating to the first.
6. **Selective base-rate quoting.** Both sides cherry-pick benchmark numbers. "Open weights are 5.7 months behind frontier" (Clark) and "open weights match frontier" (Qwen3.6-Max marketing) are both true depending on which capability you measure.

## 8. Risk / sensitivity flags

- **Tribal poles.** "Doomer" (safety camp) and "accelerationist" (a16z / e/acc) are slurs each side uses for the other. Argumend cannot use either as a node label without alienating one camp wholesale. Use "restrictionist," "open-weight pro," "pragmatic case-by-case," and "free-speech absolutist" or similar functional labels.
- **Citation balance.** The literature volume is asymmetric — the safety camp produces more long-form papers (CSET, RAND, AISI), the open-weight camp produces more op-eds and substacks. A naive citation count makes the safety camp look "more evidenced." Argumend's evidence nodes should distinguish *peer-reviewed paper* from *op-ed* from *X thread* explicitly.
- **The China question is loaded.** Anything framed as "should the US match Chinese open-weight releases" is read as either pro-China (by hawks) or pro-US-decoupling (by doves). Argumend's framing should be neutral — "what is the geostrategic effect of open-weight differential availability" — and let users move the slider.
- **Bengio + Hinton are appeal-to-authority bait.** Their endorsements *do* shift priors but should appear in evidence nodes with confidence scores reflecting they are *expert opinion*, not *evidence of mechanism*.
- **Anthropic's RSP v3.0 retreat is itself contested evidence.** Some read the Feb 2026 update as "even Anthropic is giving up on hard safety commitments"; others as "Anthropic is being more honest about what's enforceable." Argumend should map both readings.
- **Avoid endorsement.** Argumend's house position should be: the cruxes are real, the evidence is partial, the policy menu is wider than "ban or release," and reasonable people disagree on the empirical capability questions — not just the values. This is the only stance that survives both camps reading the map.
