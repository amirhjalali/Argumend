## Top 3 Recommended Topics

### #1 — AI Policy & Governance

**Why first:** AI governance is the single best fit for Argumend across all four dimensions. The cruxes are identifiable and genuinely contested among good-faith participants (open-source vs. closed models; AI in autonomous weapons; federal vs. state regulation). The Anthropic-OpenAI-Pentagon triangle is live news as of March-April 2026. The LessWrong/EA Forum/rationalist community — Argumend's natural early-adopter base — is deeply invested in these questions. Maps won't go stale for months.

**Best first argument maps:**
- "Should frontier AI models be open-sourced?" — the most active live debate, symmetric evidence landscape, named camps with cited positions
- "Should AI be used in autonomous lethal weapons systems?" — Anthropic vs. OpenAI Pentagon situation gives it immediate news hook
- "Should US federal law preempt state AI regulations?" — clean federalism crux, March 2026 framework makes it timely

**Risk mitigation:** Version-date all maps explicitly (AI moves fast). Watch for rationalist community capture — the EA prior on x-risk being severe means crowdsourced map additions may over-represent that camp.

### #2 — Emerging Niches: Psychedelics & EA/Longtermism

**Why second:** Two sub-topics here are individually top-tier fits.

**Psychedelics:** April 2026 is peak relevance — Trump executive order (April 18), FDA priority vouchers for psilocybin and methylone (April 24), Compass Pathways approaching approval. The regulatory pathway crux ("standard FDA process vs. accelerated approval, and at what evidence threshold?") is clean, empirically rich, and low-toxicity compared to most political debates. Rationalist community is heavily interested. Almost no PR downside — psychedelics therapy is broadly popular across political spectrum.

**EA longtermism vs. near-term focus:** This is the crux that most directly maps to Argumend's likely early users. LessWrong and EA Forum are the natural distribution channels. The debate — should x-risk get disproportionate philanthropic resources at the expense of near-term tractable suffering? — is exactly the kind of values + empirical hybrid that argument maps handle best. EA Forum posts already approximate Argumend's format; Argumend can offer the structured visualization layer.

**Best first argument maps:**
- "Should psychedelic-assisted therapies receive accelerated FDA approval?" — hook to April 2026 news, clean regulatory crux
- "Should AI safety receive priority over near-term global health in EA resource allocation?" — high engagement with core audience, genuine disagreement

### #3 — Education Policy (specific sub-topics)

**Why third:** Education has unusually clean crux structure across multiple politically distinct sub-debates. The school choice / federal scholarship tax credit debate (the One Big Beautiful Bill) is live legislative conflict as of April 2026. AI in classrooms (the Purdue mass-accusation incident, April 24) provides a fresh news hook. Standardized testing and student debt policy (SAVE plan collapse, March 2026) complete the portfolio.

**Audience overlap is stronger than it first appears:** Rationalist/cerebral readers are disproportionately educators, students, or academics. School choice and AI in education are topics where their epistemic training adds genuine value — these are exactly the debates where "both sides" punditry fails and structured argument mapping helps.

**Best first argument maps:**
- "Should AI-generated content be treated as academic dishonesty?" — timely (Purdue incident), clear normative crux, universal audience
- "Do school voucher programs improve educational outcomes?" — clean empirical crux, active legislative debate, 2026-specific hook
- "Does standardized testing predict college success adequately to justify its use in admissions?" — post-affirmative-action pivot makes this freshly relevant

## Topics to Deprioritize (and Why)

### Israel-Palestine (03) — DEPRIORITIZE strongly

**Score 4/10.** The topic has extreme audience toxicity: any framing choice will be read as political statement, both pro-Israeli and pro-Palestinian communities will pressure the platform, and the asymmetric emotional investment makes neutral argument mapping functionally impossible at the crux level. The conflict is also operationally complex in ways that resist clean crux structure — you need deep background to evaluate the "2,400 ceasefire violations" claim, for example.

**Exception:** The narrow policy sub-crux "Should the US condition military aid on Israeli compliance with humanitarian law?" has a cleaner structure and is less inflammatory than the meta-conflict. If tackling this space, start there and nowhere else.

### US Politics (02) — DEPRIORITIZE as a category; use as a source

**Score 5/10.** The debate energy is maximum but the Argumend-fit is poor: political debates are tribal sorting exercises rather than truth-seeking. Maps go stale in days. And the risk of partisan brand capture — being labeled "liberal" or "conservative" — is existential for a platform that needs users across the political spectrum.

**However:** Specific policy cruxes *within* US politics are high-fit — the tariff economics question, DOGE spending cuts, and executive power constitutional debates all have clean enough structure for argument maps. Build maps on specific policies, not on "US politics."

### Cultural / Identity Politics (06) — DEPRIORITIZE for now

**Score 5/10.** The rational intellectual case for tackling this is strong (genuine cruxes on DEI effectiveness, trans sports, campus speech). But the practical risks are severe: any framing immediately signals tribal membership, vulnerable populations are involved in some sub-debates, and the weaponized-cropping problem (screenshots of "the case for X" divorced from context) is worst here.

**Path to engagement:** Establish credibility on lower-stakes topics first. Return to DEI program effectiveness (corporate governance angle, less personal) and campus free speech legislation (meta-debate about speech itself) after 6-12 months of operation.

### Public Health / Vaccines (07) — DEPRIORITIZE specific sub-topics

**Score 6/10.** The debate energy is very high (RFK/HHS, measles outbreak, drug pricing) but the misinformation amplification risk is uniquely severe on vaccine safety debates. Maps that "steelman" anti-vaccine arguments get weaponized by anti-vax communities regardless of framing quality.

**Exception:** Drug pricing (MFN policy, PBM transparency) and COVID origins are substantially lower-risk sub-topics with clean empirical cruxes. Prioritize those over vaccine safety debates specifically.

## Major Surprises from the Research

**1. Psychedelics broke in real-time.** The Trump executive order (April 18) and FDA priority vouchers (April 24) happened *during* this research window. Psychedelics went from "emerging niche" to "breaking news" in 10 days. This is Argumend's rare opportunity to publish an argument map while a topic is at peak news velocity.

**2. The 1.5°C target is officially lost.** RFF's Global Energy Outlook 2026 framing ("How the World Lost the Goal of 1.5°C") represents a major Overton window shift. Climate policy debate has moved from "should we act?" to "what do we do given that conventional mitigation failed?" This makes the geoengineering and nuclear sub-debates more urgent and more tractable for Argumend than general climate advocacy.

**3. Section 230 has its most serious legislative threat ever.** The Sunset Section 230 Act (bipartisan, 10 senators, January 2027 effective date) is a bigger deal than most tech media is treating it. Combined with the *Anderson v. TikTok* decision on algorithmic recommendation liability, 2026 may be the pivotal year for the internet liability framework. Argumend has a conflict-of-interest angle here (as a platform potentially covered by Section 230) but also unique credibility.

**4. EA is rebuilding quietly.** Post-FTX, the EA community has shed its Silicon Valley hype-adjacency and is doing genuinely more rigorous intellectual work. The longtermism vs. near-term crux is producing some of the highest-quality structured argument that exists anywhere. Argumend maps in this space would find an audience pre-equipped for the format.

**5. US partisan debates are un-mappable in April 2026.** The pace and tribal intensity of current US political debate (25th Amendment letters, 254 executive orders in one term) makes structured argument mapping nearly useless as a tool — the debate isn't about understanding, it's about power. This is a clear "deprioritize" signal even though debate energy is maximum.

## Decision Framework for Next Maps

For each candidate map, ask three questions:
1. **Is there a falsifiable crux?** — not "X is good," but "If data shows Y, does that change your view on X?"
2. **Are the main camps identifiable without tribal signaling?** — can you name the camps without using words that function as team jerseys?
3. **Will the map be more useful in 30 days, less useful, or about the same?** — if "less," prioritize.

By this framework, the highest-priority maps for Argumend in May 2026 are:

1. **"Should frontier AI models be released as open source?"** (AI policy)
2. **"Should psilocybin receive accelerated FDA approval for treatment-resistant depression?"** (Psychedelics — peak timing)
3. **"Should AI x-risk receive priority over near-term global health in EA allocation?"** (EA discourse)
4. **"Does stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI) research pose unacceptable risks?"** (Climate geoengineering)
5. **"Do school voucher programs improve educational outcomes?"** (Education)
