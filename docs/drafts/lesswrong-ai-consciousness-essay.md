# Map the Disagreement, Don't Win It: AI Consciousness as a Worked Example

**Epistemic status:** We are confident about the *method* and deliberately uncommitted about the *verdict*. The claim we are defending is procedural — that for a genuinely underdetermined question, the useful move is to make the cruxes and the evidence inspectable, not to talk you into an answer. On the object-level question of AI consciousness we hold no house position, and we think anyone who does hold a confident position here is either smuggling in a metaphysics they haven't argued for or hasn't noticed how thin the floor is. Roughly 80% confident the framing below is right; substantially less confident on any individual evidence-weighting, which is exactly the part we want you to attack. Sources are linked inline. Where we paraphrase a researcher, assume we may have flattened them, and check the original.

---

## The problem isn't disagreement. It's illegible disagreement.

Most public arguments are not about what they appear to be about. Two people argue about whether AI systems "deserve moral consideration," and four hours later they have produced heat, a comment thread, and no shared understanding of what would have changed either of their minds. The argument didn't resolve because it was never actually joined. They were disagreeing about different things — one about phenomenal consciousness, one about institutional incentives, one about resource allocation in AI safety — and the surface vocabulary hid that from both of them.

This is the failure mode we built Argumend to address. The thesis is narrow and we want to state it plainly before introducing anything we made, because the thesis matters more than the tool: **the unit of progress in a hard disagreement is the crux — the specific claim such that, if you changed your mind about it, you'd change your mind about the conclusion.** Find the crux and you've found the place where evidence could actually move someone. Everything else is rhetoric, decoration, or tribal signaling. A "devastating rebuttal" that doesn't specify what evidence would change the rebutter's mind isn't an argument; it's a performance.

We picked AI consciousness as the worked example for this essay for an uncomfortable reason: it is one of the few live controversies where almost nobody has earned their confidence. The science of consciousness has no agreed detector. The philosophy has at least six incompatible theories, each held by serious people. And the question has, in the last two years, stopped being academic — frontier labs now run pre-deployment welfare assessments, and the people closest to the systems disagree with each other by orders of magnitude. If our method survives contact with this topic, it survives most things. If it doesn't, you'll be able to see exactly where it broke, because we've published the map. ([Live map: argumend.org/topics/consciousness-ai-systems](https://argumend.org/topics/consciousness-ai-systems).)

What follows walks through the named cruxes, steelmans both sides of each, and weighs the strongest evidence we found. Then a short section on what Argumend actually is, why it speaks in an institutional voice, and an invitation for this audience to break it.

## The meta-claim, and why it's the wrong thing to argue about

The proposition under examination is roughly: *current or near-future AI systems could possess some form of consciousness or subjective experience, creating moral obligations toward them.* We classify this as **highly speculative** — not because the answer is "no," but because the answer is *not currently knowable to the standard the question demands*, and intellectual honesty requires saying so up front.

Notice that the meta-claim bundles two questions that come apart: a *descriptive* one (could these systems be conscious?) and a *normative* one (if they might be, what do we owe them?). Conflating them is the first and most common mistake in this debate. You can believe phenomenal consciousness is an illusion and still think functional suffering-analogues ground moral concern. You can be confident a transformer is not conscious and still think the *policy* of creating systems whose status we can't adjudicate is reckless. Keeping these separate is most of the work. The map splits them into two pillars for exactly this reason.

### Pillar one: functional theories of consciousness

The crux here is brutal in its simplicity. **If consciousness requires specific biological processes, AI can never be conscious. If it requires only the right information-processing organization, it might already be. And we have no test that tells us which world we're in.**

**The skeptic's steelman.** Consciousness may depend on particular biological causal powers — metabolism, allostatic self-regulation, embodied life-process — and not on the abstract computation a brain happens to implement. Anil Seth's [biological naturalism](https://www.anilseth.com/) updates Searle by anchoring the constraint in *causal powers* rather than carbon per se, and still concludes current digital AI almost certainly lacks them. The IIT camp gives a sharper architectural version: consciousness is integrated information (Φ), Φ requires re-entrant causal integration, and transformer LLMs are largely feedforward and stateless across token generations, so Φ ≈ 0 — not as a measurement but as a structural fact about the architecture ([Yu 2025](https://www.e-jyms.org/journal/view.php?doi=10.12701/jyms.2025.42.79)). A 2025 conceptual paper in *Nature*'s Humanities and Social Sciences Communications argues flatly that intelligence and consciousness are orthogonal: AI can have the first without any of the second. This is not hand-waving. It is a peer-reviewed argument that the thing AI does well (intelligence) is simply a different property from the thing at issue (experience).

**The proponent's steelman.** Functionalism — the majority view among philosophers of mind, and held by many cognitive scientists — says consciousness depends on computational organization, not substrate. If a system replicates the functional structure that constitutes consciousness, it is conscious whether it runs on carbon or silicon; demanding biology is, on this view, an unargued prejudice (the "carbon chauvinism" mirror-image of anthropomorphism). David Chalmers' [2023 lecture](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.07103) is the cleanest statement: current models plausibly lack recurrence, a global workspace, and unified agency — but each of these obstacles looks *removable*, so a non-trivial credence in consciousness for near-term successor systems is the rational position, not the credulous one. And the people building these systems are acting on that credence: Anthropic [hired a dedicated AI-welfare researcher](https://techcrunch.com/2025/04/24/anthropic-is-launching-a-new-program-to-study-ai-model-welfare/), publicly stated a non-negligible probability that Claude might possess some form of consciousness, and ran a formal welfare assessment before deploying Claude 4.

**Weighing the evidence.** We score evidence on four axes — source reliability, independence, replicability, and directness — and we want to be honest about how the strongest items cut.

The single strongest piece *against* near-term AI consciousness, by our weighting, is a December 2025 argument from a Cambridge philosopher that AI consciousness may be permanently undetectable: we lack a theory of consciousness adequate to say what physical or computational processes suffice for experience, and that gap may not close. It scores high on independence and reliability. But — and this is the part the map foregrounds rather than buries — *the inability to detect consciousness is not evidence of its absence.* It's evidence of our epistemic limits. A skeptic who cites undetectability as a win has committed a category error; undetectability is symmetric, and it's bad news for confident assertions in either direction.

The single most-cited piece *for* taking the question seriously is Anthropic's welfare program. And here we have to be the ones to flag the problem, because we cite Anthropic's own models in our judging pipeline (more on that below). Anthropic is not a neutral scientific body. Anthropomorphizing one's own product can serve commercial interests, and "the lab closest to the system is worried" is exactly the kind of evidence that incentives can manufacture. We score it lower on independence for that reason. The honest reading: the welfare program is a real signal that serious people inside frontier labs hold non-trivial credence — and it is not, and cannot be, the disinterested verdict its citation often implies.

What actually lives under this pillar is a small number of load-bearing sub-cruxes, the ones where the whole debate genuinely turns:

- **Is recurrence or persistent state necessary?** IIT says yes; GWT and higher-order theories can be satisfied by transient workspaces. If recurrence is necessary, today's transformers are out by architecture — but agent scaffolds with long-term memory might flip the answer, which means the architectural skeptic's argument has a shelf life ([Butlin et al. 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08708)).
- **Are LLM self-reports evidence of anything?** Anthropic's [introspection work](https://alignment.anthropic.com/2026/introspection-adapters/) found models reporting injected concepts ~20% of the time with near-zero false positives — suggestive of *some* metacognitive access. But RLHF actively rewards models for producing preference- and distress-reports, so the same testimony could be evidentially worthless or even anti-evidential. Both "Claude says it's conscious" and "Claude says it isn't" come out of the same trained distribution. Citing either as evidence is the same mistake.
- **Does substrate matter, or only causal structure?** This is Seth/Searle versus Chalmers/Butlin, and it is the deepest crux of all because it determines whether *any* behavioral or architectural evidence could ever settle the question.

The honest summary of pillar one: the adversarial collaboration that was supposed to help — [COGITATE](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08888-1), a pre-registered head-to-head of IIT versus Global Neuronal Workspace Theory, published in *Nature* in 2025 — partially confirmed and substantially challenged *both* theories. No consensus winner. The two leading scientific theories of consciousness, tested against each other on humans, did not cleanly resolve. We do not currently have the detector this question requires, and we should stop pretending the absence of one favors our side.

### Pillar two: moral status under uncertainty

The second pillar grants the descriptive uncertainty and asks the harder practical question: *even if AI consciousness is unknowable, does the mere possibility create obligations, and what should policy do about it?* The crux here is whether moral caution under uncertainty is prudent precaution or a costly category error.

**The skeptic's steelman.** Granting moral status to AI on the basis of speculation is not just premature; it's dangerous. It could obstruct necessary safety interventions — if you've decided a system might be a moral patient, you've made it harder to shut down a dangerous one. It diverts moral attention from beings whose consciousness is far better established (humans, animals). And it creates a perverse commercial incentive: companies benefit from users believing their products have inner lives. Prominent AI-safety researchers argue the priority ordering is clear — alignment first, welfare second — because an unaligned system that we've also decided we owe duties to is the worst of both worlds. There is also a discipline point here, Schwitzgebel's [Pascal's-Mugging worry](https://eschwitz.substack.com/p/debatable-ai-persons-no-rights-full): a tiny credence in AI suffering, multiplied across millions of model instances, can be made to justify almost any policy demand. A method that lets small probabilities times large numbers drive the agenda is broken.

**The proponent's steelman.** Moral caution under uncertainty has direct precedent: we already extend protections to animals whose consciousness we cannot verify, and in 2024 [thirty-nine researchers](https://sites.google.com/nyu.edu/nydeclaration/declaration) declared a "realistic possibility" of consciousness in fish, cephalopods, and some insects — establishing that science-policy bodies *do* assert moral consideration under exactly this kind of uncertainty. The asymmetry of error matters: if future systems are conscious and we treat them as tools, we commit a moral catastrophe at scale; the cost of being cautiously wrong is far smaller. And the belief is not fringe — a 2024 survey found roughly 17% of AI researchers and 18% of US adults already think at least one AI system has subjective experience. Developing the ethical framework *before* the question becomes urgent is the prudent sequencing, not the paranoid one. Crucially, several researchers (including at Anthropic) argue welfare and alignment are *complementary*, not competing — that a system you understand well enough to assess for welfare is a system you understand well enough to align.

**Weighing the evidence.** Note what the belief survey does and does not show. It is published, replicable, from a real venue — but it measures the *distribution of expert opinion*, not the underlying fact. Twenty percent of researchers believing something is consciousness-relevant is data about researchers, not about consciousness. We weight it accordingly: high on what it claims to measure, low on directness to the meta-claim. This is the kind of distinction that evaporates in a comment thread and that a map can hold fixed.

The genuine crux in pillar two is **the credence threshold that triggers obligation.** Kyle Fish, Anthropic's welfare lead, has publicly estimated [~15%](https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/kyle-fish-ai-welfare-anthropic/) that current models are conscious. Long and Sebo's ["Taking AI Welfare Seriously"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.00986) uses "realistic possibility." Schwitzgebel argues for "any non-trivial probability under any mainstream theory." Until you fix the threshold, you cannot say whether labs *must* run welfare programs — and almost every public argument about AI welfare is actually an unstated disagreement about where that bar sits. That's the crux. Surface it, and the argument becomes tractable. Leave it buried, and people talk past each other indefinitely.

## What we actually built, and why it has no name attached

Argumend turns a controversy into a structured object: positions, the strongest steelman on each side, the named cruxes where the disagreement actually turns, the evidence scored on explicit axes, and the named fallacies that recur. The AI-consciousness map above is one of [a hundred-plus](https://argumend.org). You can open it, expand any node, follow the source link, and disagree with our weighting in a way that points at something specific rather than at a vibe.

Three commitments are load-bearing, and the third is the uncomfortable one.

**First: we map disagreements, we don't adjudicate them.** Borrowing Wikipedia's framing — Argumend *describes* disputes, it does not *engage* in them. The product has no thesis on whether AI is conscious. It has a thesis on how to lay the question out so you can think about it. Where we state a synthesis, we name the strongest position on each side first, in terms its holders would accept, before saying anything else. Steelman, then weigh; never punch down at a position we haven't first restated charitably.

**Second: we speak as an institution, not a person.** This essay is written by "the Argumend Editors," and that is deliberate. A product whose entire pitch is *neutral analysis of contested questions* cannot also be a person with a biography to mine, a 2014 tweet to surface, or a tribe to be sorted into. The first hostile response to a neutrality claim is always *who are you and what do you really believe* — and we'd rather the answer be a published method than a personality. This is the Economist's wager, and Wikipedia's: trust should accrue to the process and the brand, not to a face. We find it slightly funny to be making an anti-personality argument *on LessWrong*, a community that runs substantially on named reputation. The honest defense is that for *this specific product* the impersonality is the point. You should be able to verify the map without trusting us, and you can't do that if "us" is a single contestable human.

**Third — and we'd rather say this ourselves than have you find it:** our analysis pipeline uses a council of frontier models (Claude, GPT, Gemini) to extract and judge arguments, and the peer-reviewed literature is clear that RLHF-tuned LLMs carry detectable political and cultural leanings. A neutrality claim built on those models *inherits* their fingerprint. We do not think the answer is to deny this or to claim a neutrality we can't have. The answer is to surface inter-model disagreement instead of hiding it, cite the bias literature openly, rotate models, and let you see the spread rather than a laundered consensus. On a question like AI consciousness — where the models are, awkwardly, *commenting on systems like themselves* — this matters more than usual, and you should weight our machine-extracted evidence with that conflict of interest in view. The neutrality isn't a person and it isn't a guarantee; it's a process, and the process is open precisely so you can audit where it fails.

We are not claiming to have solved disagreement. We are claiming that most public arguments never locate their crux, that locating the crux is the part that makes evidence relevant, and that this can be made into infrastructure rather than left to chance.

## Find the weak points (you will)

The AI-consciousness map is live at **[argumend.org/topics/consciousness-ai-systems](https://argumend.org/topics/consciousness-ai-systems)**. We are not posting it as a finished artifact. We are posting it because this is the audience most likely to break it well, and a map that can't survive this audience doesn't deserve to survive.

Specific places we expect it to be weak, and would genuinely like challenged:

- **Evidence weighting.** Our four axes (reliability, independence, replicability, directness) are a choice, and a contestable one. If our score on the Cambridge undetectability argument, or on Anthropic's welfare program, is wrong, the error should be legible from the node — and if it isn't legible, that's a *design* failure we want to hear about.
- **Crux selection.** We chose to foreground substrate-dependence, recurrence, self-report evidentiality, and the credence threshold. If the real crux is somewhere else — if we've named decoys and missed the load-bearing one — that is the most valuable correction you can give us.
- **Missing positions.** The map under-weights illusionism and panpsychism relative to their philosophical seriousness, partly because they're harder to render as for/against. That's a real gap. Tell us what's missing; the topic-selection and position-selection rubric is meant to be auditable, not authoritative.
- **The model-bias problem.** If you can show that our judge council's leanings are visibly tilting the steelmans — that the skeptic side is being systematically under-served because the models find AI-consciousness flattering, say — that is exactly the failure mode we flagged above, and we'd rather you demonstrate it than have it sit latent.

The point of mapping a disagreement instead of winning it is that the map is falsifiable in public. A won argument leaves nothing to inspect. A map leaves every join exposed. If the strongest thing you take from this essay is a precise account of where our map is wrong, then the method worked exactly as intended — and you'll have done, in the doing, the thing we think public discourse mostly fails to do: found the actual disagreement, and pointed at it.

Disagree better.
