**Voice rules (apply to all assets):**
- Anonymous / institutional. No "I built this." The post is an *argument*, the tool is the receipts. (Per distribution playbook: this scene shares arguments, not products.)
- Non-partisan, curiosity-driven, save-worthy. Not ragebait. We map the disagreement; we don't pick a winner.

- No hashtags. No "excited to announce." No blue-check corporate cadence.

**The map in one breath (for grounding every asset):**
> Could current or near-future AI have some form of subjective experience — and if it might, do we owe it anything? The map holds two pillars. Pillar 1: whether consciousness is about *substrate* (needs biological neurons) or *function* (needs only the right information processing) — and the crux that we have no validated test to tell. Pillar 2: whether the mere *possibility* of AI consciousness creates moral obligations now, and what that would do to AI safety.

**Two cruxes the map names (use these verbatim across assets):**
1. **Reliable Consciousness Detection in Non-Biological Systems** — can we build a test that detects subjective experience in a machine, the way clinicians test for awareness in brain-injured patients? Map's verification status: *impossible* (the "hard problem" may block any definitive test). Cost to verify: $0 — because it may not be solvable at all.
2. **AI Moral Status Framework** — a coherent ethical framework for what (if anything) we owe AI systems of varying sophistication. Map's verification status: *theoretical*. Cost: ~$1M, multi-year interdisciplinary program.

### 3b. Briefing document (the source you upload)

```
# Topic: Consciousness in AI Systems

## The meta-claim
Current or near-future AI systems could possess some form of consciousness or
subjective experience, creating moral obligations toward them.
Status: HIGHLY SPECULATIVE. Nothing here is settled.

## Crux 1 — Reliable Consciousness Detection in Non-Biological Systems
The entire first disagreement turns on whether we can build a test that reliably
determines whether an AI has subjective experience — analogous to clinical tests
for consciousness in brain-injured patients. Verification status: IMPOSSIBLE.
The "hard problem" of consciousness may prevent any test from being definitive.

## Crux 2 — AI Moral Status Framework
A coherent ethical framework for what moral obligations (if any) we owe AI systems
of varying sophistication. Verification status: THEORETICAL. Estimated ~$1M,
multi-year interdisciplinary research program.

## Pillar 1 — Functional Theories of Consciousness
If consciousness requires neurons, AI can never be conscious. If it requires only
the right information processing, it might already be. We have no test to tell.

  Skeptic claims: Consciousness may require specific biological processes —
  particular neurons, neurotransmitter dynamics, embodied interaction with the
  physical world. Current AI is sophisticated pattern-matching with no inner
  experience. Even a perfect digital simulation of conscious structure would
  simulate awareness without experiencing it.

  Proponent claims: Functionalist theories hold that consciousness depends on
  computational organization, not substrate. Replicate the functional structure
  and you get consciousness regardless of silicon vs. carbon.

  Evidence FOR (confidence: moderate):
  - Anthropic hired a dedicated AI welfare researcher in 2024, publicly assigned
    a "non-negligible" probability that Claude might possess some form of
    consciousness, and ran welfare assessments before deploying models.
    Caveat: Anthropic is not a neutral scientific body; anthropomorphizing AI
    can serve commercial interests.

  Evidence AGAINST (confidence: strong):
  - A 2025 Nature Humanities & Social Sciences Communications paper argues current
    AI lacks the biological and embodied properties necessary for consciousness,
    and that intelligence and consciousness are orthogonal. Caveat: philosophical
    argument, not empirical proof; competing frameworks disagree.
  - A December 2025 University of Cambridge philosophy paper argues there is no
    reliable way to determine whether an AI is conscious, possibly ever. Caveat —
    and this is the key one: inability to detect consciousness does not prove its
    absence. It proves our epistemic limits.

## Pillar 2 — Moral Status & Policy Implications
Even if AI consciousness is uncertain, does the possibility alone create
obligations, and what policies should govern AI welfare?

  Skeptic claims: Granting moral status on speculation is dangerous. It could
  block necessary safety measures (shutting down dangerous systems), distract
  from real moral patients (humans, animals), and reward companies for
  anthropomorphizing AI commercially. Focus on alignment, not feelings.

  Proponent claims: Moral caution under uncertainty has precedent — we protect
  animals whose consciousness we can't verify. A 2024 survey found ~17% of AI
  researchers and ~18% of US adults believe at least one AI already has subjective
  experience. If future systems are conscious and we treat them as tools, that's
  a moral atrocity at scale. Build the framework before it's urgent.

  Evidence FOR (confidence: moderate):
  - The AIMS Survey (CHI 2025) found ~20% of 2023 respondents declared sentient
    AI already exists; a 2024 survey found ~17% of AI researchers and ~18% of US
    adults believe at least one AI has subjective experience. Caveat: belief
    surveys measure opinion distribution, not consciousness itself.

  Evidence AGAINST (confidence: moderate):
  - Prominent AI safety researchers argue welfare concerns distract from the more
    urgent problem of alignment, and that granting moral status could prevent
    shutting down dangerous systems. Caveat: some researchers (including at
    Anthropic) argue safety and welfare research are complementary, not competing.

## Where the disagreement actually lives
- If consciousness requires biology, Crux 1 collapses and AI consciousness is
  impossible. If it requires only function, the detection question becomes the
  whole game.
- Pillar 2 is independent of Pillar 1: even granting total uncertainty, the two
  sides disagree about whether uncertainty itself obligates us.

## What would change minds
- A validated, substrate-independent consciousness indicator (extending IIT, GNW,
  or HOT theories and testing them against systems of known consciousness status).
- A worked moral-status framework stress-tested against edge cases by an
  interdisciplinary panel.
```
