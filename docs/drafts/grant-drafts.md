### Project summary

Argumend ([argumend.org](https://www.argumend.org)) turns public controversies into structured, interactive maps of positions, evidence, cruxes, confidence levels, and logical fallacies. The goal is not to make people agree — it is to make the disagreement *legible* enough that people can reason about it. Each map is built from primary sources, marks where the debate actually turns (the crux), steelmans both sides, and surfaces inter-model disagreement via a multi-model judge council. This is epistemic infrastructure: a reference layer that makes the structure of an argument inspectable, citable, and embeddable — the way Our World in Data did for statistics and Metaculus did for forecasts.

The product exists and is live; the bottleneck is content depth, verification, and distribution — not engineering. A regrant funds three concrete workstreams over ~3 months:

## 2. ACX Grants

### What it is

Argumend ([argumend.org](https://www.argumend.org)) is an epistemics tool for public controversies. It takes a contested question — "Is AI consciousness possible?", "Does geoengineering reduce or increase risk?", "Is nuclear power the safest path to decarbonization?" — and renders it as an interactive map: the positions, the evidence behind each, the *crux* (the load-bearing claim where the debate actually turns), confidence scores, and the logical fallacies in play. Both sides are steelmanned. A multi-model judge council scores each claim and surfaces where the models disagree.

The promise is not "we'll tell you who's right." It's *disagree better*: make the disagreement structured enough that a reader can find the real crux instead of arguing past it. There are `[METRIC: ~114 — verify]` maps live today.

### Why it matters

Short-form algorithms reward partisan intensity; the 2026 research is unambiguous that the dominant channels of public discourse select for heat, not light. Argumend is a deliberate counter-bet: an artifact optimized for *saves, citations, and embeds* rather than likes. If it works, it becomes a small piece of epistemic infrastructure — a reference layer the way Our World in Data is for statistics or Metaculus is for forecasts. Every comparable epistemic public good (LessWrong, Metaculus, Our World in Data, Asterisk) is subsidized rather than commercial, because the value is civic, not extractable. ACX Grants exists precisely to seed work like this: high-variance, mission-aligned, undervalued by markets.

The wedge is concrete. The rationalist / EA / AI-safety community already speaks the native vocabulary (crux, steelman, calibration) and already needs a way to publish the structure of a debate in a form others can cite and embed. Win that one community with a few canonical maps, and the format compounds through embeddability and LLM citation.

### What the money does

The product is built and live; the gap is depth, verification, and reach — all of which are token- and content-bound, not engineering-bound. Funding buys ~3 months of:

1. **Flagship maps** on live high-stakes questions (AI consciousness / model welfare first), each anchored to a long-form essay in ACX/LW register.
2. **Primary-source verification** — every claim traced to a source, link rot flagged, extractable summaries and named studies/dates added so the maps are genuinely trustworthy and LLM-citable.
3. **Distribution** — embeddable CC-BY iframe maps, a LessWrong/EA Forum launch, and multi-format packaging (short video, audio, threads) so the work reaches readers where they already are.

This is the cheapest possible test of the core thesis: does a canonical, verified, well-distributed map move a named community?
